## I. Core Thesis

> **"The long-term existence of one or several small pieces of Red political power surrounded by a White regime—this is a thing never before seen anywhere in the world."**
> — Mao Zedong, *Why Can China's Red Political Power Exist?* (1928)

AI-era transformation faces the same structural problem: **Nearly 100% of global AI infrastructure is controlled by monopoly capital—data on their servers, chips in their data centers, models in their parameters. How can data common ownership start from zero?**

The answer: **Build base areas.**

Not "overthrow the old system first, then build the new"—but build "strongholds" of the new system within the **gaps** of the old system. Let the new system operate locally, prove its feasibility, then expand.

This document's objective: design the **first base area** for data common ownership—where to build it, how to build it, how to sustain it, how to expand it.

## II. Why Base Areas?

### 2.1 Proposals without base areas are empty talk

Document 07 starts with the "Global Algorithm Convention"—top-down.
Document 09 starts with the "International Governance Framework"—big-to-small.

Common problem: **neither explains the first step.**

> In the early days of the Chinese Revolution, there were also two tendencies:
> - "City-center" — capture big cities first, then expand to countryside (copied from the USSR)
> - "Simultaneous uprising" — launch everywhere at once, directly overthrow the old regime
> - Practice proved both wrong—the correct path was "countryside encircles cities"
>
> Why? Not because the countryside had magic—but because:
> 1. Enemy power was concentrated in big cities—head-on battles were unwinnable
> 2. Enemy rule was weakest in the countryside—space to survive
> 3. The rural population was the overwhelming majority—mass base
> 4. New political power could be established locally—prove the new system in small areas first

The same logic applies to the AI era.

### 2.2 Where is the "countryside" of the AI era?

"Countryside" is not a geographic concept—it is a **structural concept**: areas where **enemy power is weak, mass needs are urgent, and alternative institutions can be established locally**.

| Dimension | AI-era "city" (enemy strong) | AI-era "countryside" (breakthrough possible) |
|-----------|------------------------------|----------------------------------------------|
| Industry | Search, social media, e-commerce, cloud computing | Public health data sharing, agricultural data collaboration, public education data |
| Geography | Silicon Valley, Beijing, Shenzhen | Global South secondary cities, smaller European countries |
| Data | User behavior data (locked on platforms) | Public data (government, research, meteorological) |
| Application | Chatbots, content recommendation | Public services (transport, environment, health monitoring) |

## III. Where is Jinggangshan?

### 3.1 Selection Criteria

Based on the experience of selecting Jinggangshan (the Luoxiao Mountains), a good base area must satisfy five conditions:

```
1. Weak link in enemy rule
   → Areas not deeply penetrated by monopoly capital
   → Or areas where alternative solutions can already survive

2. Good mass base
   → Urgent social needs that existing capital solutions cannot meet
   → Mass willingness to participate in change

3. Sustainable economic model
   → Can sustain and develop itself
   → Does not depend on external funding (or only limited early-stage dependence)

4. "Terrain" advantage
   → Institutional protection space (legal gaps, local government support)
   → Technical defensibility (open-source architecture, distributed deployment)

5. Scalability
   → Model can be replicated and promoted
   → Not a one-time experiment
```

### 3.2 Three Candidate Base Areas

#### Candidate 1: Latin American Public Health Data Trust

**Location:** Chile or Brazil

**Why here:**
- Latin American countries have strong "digital sovereignty" political will—Chile is drafting data protection law, Brazil has LGPD
- Public health is "low-sensitivity, high-need"—nobody opposes "using AI to diagnose disease"
- Chinese/Huawei infrastructure presence in Latin America—submarine cables, data centers (but must be locally led)
- Population size appropriate (Chile 19 million)—good for a pilot

**Specific plan:**
```
Establish "National Public Health Data Public Trust":

Data sources:
  → Anonymized诊疗 data from public hospitals
  → Public health surveillance data (infectious diseases, chronic diseases)
  → Voluntarily contributed health behavior data from residents

Data uses:
  → Train localized AI diagnosis models (for Latin American common diseases—dengue, Chagas, tuberculosis)
  → Optimize public health resource allocation
  → Pharmaceutical R&D (new drugs using data without payment must have pricing constrained by the trust)

Governance structure:
  → Council: government 1/4 + medical institution reps 1/4 + citizen reps 1/4 + technical experts 1/4
  → Revenue distribution: AI-generated economic benefits → 50% back to public health system, 30% to data contributors (data dividend), 20% operating costs

Success indicators:
  → Within 2 years: cover 50%+ of public hospital data
  → At least 1 AI diagnosis model deployed (accuracy >90%)
  > Voluntary resident participation rate >30%
  → Prove data common ownership is more efficient—models trained on shared data are X% more accurate than single-hospital models
```

**Risks:**
| Risk | Response |
|------|----------|
| US pressure ("data commonization is confiscation") | Emphasize "public trust" not "confiscation"—analogous to public libraries |
| Political transition disrupts policy | Write into law not executive order; seek cross-party support |
| Insufficient technical capacity | Partner with local universities; use open-source AI tools |
| Poor data quality | Phased approach—start with high-quality tertiary hospital data, then expand |

#### Candidate 2: European City Transport Data Cooperative

**Location:** A German city (e.g., Hamburg) or Dutch city (e.g., Utrecht)

**Why here:**
- EU has the world's strongest data protection legislation (GDPR) and is drafting the Data Governance Act—most favorable legal environment
- European cities have strong municipal governance traditions—transport is directly controlled by city government
- Europe has cooperative legal frameworks—data cooperatives have clear legal status

**Specific plan:**
```
Establish "City Transport Data Cooperative":

Data sources:
  → Voluntary GPS travel data from citizens (via app)
  → Public transit system operational data
  → Municipal sensor traffic data

Data uses:
  → Optimize public transit routes and schedules
  → Reduce congestion (AI traffic light调度)
  → Provide data for urban planning

Governance structure:
  → Cooperative members (data contributors), one person one vote
  → Elected management committee
  → Any external entity (commercial company) using data must pay, revenue belongs to cooperative members

Difference from capitalist solutions:
  → Uber/Waze: collect data → optimize navigation → sell ads/sell data → profit to shareholders
  → Cooperative: collect citizen data → optimize transport → improve public services → benefit to citizens
  → Nearly same technology, completely different ownership and distribution
```

#### Candidate 3: Chinese City "Delivery Rider Data Cooperative"

**Location:** A Chinese second-tier city (e.g., Changsha, Chengdu)

**Why here:**
- China has the world's largest gig worker population—Meituan alone has 7 million+ delivery riders
- China's digital infrastructure is the most developed—but all data belongs to platforms
- Rider exploitation is among the most extreme globally—platform commission up to 20-30%, no labor protection
- China has a union legal framework (though no independent unions currently), "collective consultation" is legally permissible

**Specific plan:**
```
Establish "Rider Data Cooperative" (starting simplest):

Step 1: Joint monitoring
  → Riders collectively record and analyze platform algorithm rule changes
  → Build an "algorithm rule tracking database"—when platform changes rules, everyone analyzes together
  → This is not "technical confrontation," it's "information symmetry"

Step 2: Collective bargaining
  → When riders have platform algorithm rule data, they gain bargaining power
  → "If per-order pay drops below X, everyone goes offline for 15 minutes"
  → Data makes collective action possible

Step 3: Own platform
  → Rider cooperative launches its own dispatch app
  → Early stage: start with "non-peak hours"—orders platforms don't want (late-night delivery, long-distance)
  → Expand gradually
  → Cooperative commission at 5% (for operating costs), rest to riders

Success indicators:
  → Within 12 months: 1000+ riders covered
  → Cooperative platform dispatch efficiency不低于 commercial platforms
  → Rider income increases 20%+ (from commission reduction alone)
  → Data ownership truly in riders' hands
```

## IV. Base Area Economic Model

A base area survives only if it **can support itself economically**.

### 4.1 Revenue Sources

The data trust must be economically self-sustaining—no need for external subsidies.

```
Early stage (1-2 years):
  Startup funding: public funds (government grants, research grants) or philanthropic foundations
  Small scale, low cost—a city-level data trust, servers + staff, ~$0.5-2 million/year

Medium stage (2-5 years):
  Main revenue: data usage license fees
  Insurance companies (using health data to optimize products) → pay fees
  Pharmaceutical companies (using medical data for R&D) → pay fees
  Urban planning departments (using transport data) → pay fees (government budget)
  → Commercial user fees sufficient to cover operating costs

Mature stage (5+ years):
  Data dividend distribution—surplus beyond operating costs distributed to data contributors
  This proves "data common ownership is sustainable"
```

### 4.2 Distribution Mechanism

```
Revenue distribution formula:

Annual total revenue = Σ(commercial user data license fees) + public budget support

Distribution priority:
  First: Operating costs (servers, bandwidth, staff)—~30%
  Second: Public reserve fund (technology upgrades, risk response)—~20%
  Third: Data dividend (distributed to data contributors)—~50%

Individual data dividend distribution:
  Base share (60%): equally to all data contributors
    —Because the nature of data is social, not individual
    —Individual data value alone is minimal, collective value is enormous
  Contribution share (40%): floating based on data quantity/quality
    —Necessary incentive—otherwise no one would contribute more data
```

## V. Base Area Organizational Model

### 5.1 Governance Principles

```
Democratic centralism applied to data common ownership:

Democracy:
  → Data contributors ("data citizens") elect council by one-person-one-vote
  → Major decisions (data licensing, distribution ratios, rule changes) must go to general vote
  → Algorithm transparency—all data usage records visible to members

Centralism:
  → Council responsible for daily management decisions
  → Technical team has professional autonomy (not "every line of code must be voted on")
  → Once a decision is made, it must be executed—this guarantees efficiency

Key: balance between democracy and centralism
  → No democracy = new form of "data bureaucracy"
  → No centralism = inefficiency, unable to make any decisions
```

## VI. From Base Area to Nationwide — Expansion Path

### 6.1 Horizontal Expansion: From 1 to 10

When one data trust pilot has operated successfully for 2-3 years, begin replication:

```
Replication strategy:

Step 1: Document — turn all pilot processes, technical solutions, legal documents into "open-source toolkit"
  Next city doesn't need to start from zero
  Lower replication cost

Step 2: Training —第一批 pilot operators become "seed instructors"
  Go to next city to help build new pilot
  Experience is the best textbook

Step 3: Networking — establish data sharing agreements between trusts
  Data trusts connect—data flows across larger area, greater value
  Form "Data Trust Alliance"—共同 bargaining, standards, governance

Step 4: Standardization — develop "National Data Trust Standards" based on alliance experience
  From "各自探索" to "unified standards"
  Provide practical basis for national-level legislation
```

### 6.2 Vertical Deepening: From Low-Sensitivity to High-Sensitivity

```
Expanding domains:

Phase 1: Public data (government open data, meteorological data, research data)
  → Easiest—governments have motivation to open data
  → High quality, low controversy
  → Build trust and technical foundation

Phase 2: Livelihood data (health data, transport data, education data)
  → Closely related to people's daily lives
  → Highest sense of achievement from success
  → One disease screening model saving lives is more powerful than 100 articles

Phase 3: Economic data (consumption data, supply chain data, financial data)
  → Touches core interests—these are the real "means of production"
  → At this stage, capital已经开始 to fight back
  → Now requires political power, not technical power, to advance

Phase 4: Algorithm management power
  → Not just "your data belongs to you" but "your labor process belongs to you"
  → The most thorough transformation
```

## VII. Mistakes to Avoid

### 7.1 Historical Lessons

**Lesson 1: Failure of 1927 city uprisings**
- Launching worker armed uprisings in Shanghai, Changsha
- All failed—enemy rule was too strong in cities
- Lesson: **Challenging the enemy where they are strongest is not bravery, it's recklessness**

**Applied to AI era:**
- Don't try to compete head-on with Google, OpenAI at the start
- Don't try to "overthrow AI capitalism"
- Build base areas where the enemy doesn't pay attention (public data, health data, agricultural data)

**Lesson 2: Wang Ming's "Left" adventurism**
- Demanding the Red Army attack big cities and fight the Kuomintang's main force
- Result: Fifth anti-encirclement campaign failed, forced into the Long March
- Lesson: **When the balance of forces is unfavorable, don't fight positional warfare—fight guerrilla warfare**

**Lesson 3: Zhang Guotao's分裂主义**
- During the Long March, disobeyed central command, set up separate central committee
- Result:分裂 weakened revolutionary force
- Lesson: **Unity of overall strategy is necessary—分散行动不等于各自为政**

### 7.2 Mistakes to Avoid Now

```
Mistake 1: Waiting for the perfect plan
  "Theory isn't complete enough, let's think through every detail before acting"
  → There will never be a perfect plan
  → Practice is the sole criterion for truth—start first, improve in practice

Mistake 2: Rushing to expand
  "This pilot is good, let's promote it nationwide immediately"
  → More haste, less speed—without solid foundation, expansion leads to collapse
  → First dig deep roots in one place, then replicate

Mistake 3: Working behind closed doors
  "We design, build, and operate it ourselves"
  → Organization that doesn't integrate with local masses is doomed
  → Data trust must be "the people's own project" from day one

Mistake 4: Technological determinism
  "A good technical solution will naturally attract users"
  → Core of public trust is not technology (though technology matters)
  → Core is trust, governance, distribution—these are social problems, not technical problems

Mistake 5: Pessimism
  "What use is this small scale? It won't change the big picture."
  → A single spark can start a prairie fire
  → Every successful pilot is the best refutation of "there is no alternative"
```

## VIII. Conclusion: From Jinggangshan to the Whole World

In 1927, the Autumn Harvest Uprising on the Hunan-Jiangxi border failed. Mao Zedong led the remaining troops up Jinggangshan.

No one could have imagined then that this remote mountain base area would eventually become the starting point for the victory of the Chinese Revolution.

The same applies to the AI era. A public health data trust established today in a Latin American city—small-scale, limited impact, could be shut down at any time—but it represents a possibility: **an alternative beyond AI monopoly capital.**

> **"Marxists are not fortune-tellers. They can only indicate the general direction of future development and change, not mechanically prescribe the day and hour."**
> — Mao Zedong, *A Single Spark Can Start a Prairie Fire*

But the general direction is clear:

```
One data trust → ten data trusts → one hundred data trusts
One city → one country → one continent
Public health → livelihood data → economic data
Economic experiment → political force → systemic transformation
```

> **"The whole country is covered with dry tinder and will soon burst into flame."**

The AI era is the same—no, the **whole world** is covered with dry tinder. AI replacement unemployment, data exploitation, algorithmic alienation—these contradictions are accumulating. What we lack is not "conditions" but a single **spark**.

> **"A single spark can start a prairie fire."**

## Appendix: Base Area Construction Checklist

### Preparation Stage
- [ ] Is the location correct? (enemy weak + mass demand + political space)
- [ ] Are there at least 3 full-time organizers?
- [ ] Has the "investigation and research" of local data been completed?
- [ ] Has informal support from at least one local government been obtained?
- [ ] Is there operating funding for at least 6 months of experimentation?

### Launch Stage
- [ ] Has the first data citizens assembly been held (attendance >100)?
- [ ] Has the council been elected?
- [ ] Has the data trust charter been published (local language + English)?
- [ ] Has the open-source data management platform been deployed?
- [ ] Has the first data usage agreement been signed?

### Operations Stage
- [ ] Is there a clear revenue distribution mechanism operating transparently?
- [ ] Is there an independent algorithm oversight committee?
- [ ] Is data contributor count growing continuously (>10% monthly)?
- [ ] Is there at least 1 AI application with actual output?
- [ ] Is satisfaction among covered groups >70% (regular survey)?

### Expansion Stage
- [ ] Has the toolkit been documented?
- [ ] Are there at least 2 "seed instructors" ready to deploy to new pilots?
- [ ] Has an alliance relationship been established with at least 1 other pilot?
- [ ] Has at least 1 legislative proposal favorable to data common ownership been advanced?
- [ ] Has media attention been achieved (at least 1 mainstream media in-depth story)?
