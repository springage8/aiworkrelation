# Strong Government and a Data Federation

The AI age requires strong central authority — fragmented algorithmic governance is weak and ineffective.

## Data Federation

Data is like currency; it should be managed by a unified federal government. In *The Federalist Papers*, I argued that a loose confederation cannot govern a large nation. Today, loose data governance cannot manage a society dominated by algorithms.

**National Computing Bank** — Establish a computing power reserve management institution similar to a central bank. Computing power is the most scarce resource — if entirely priced by the market and monopolized by giants, small and medium enterprises and public institutions will never obtain sufficient computing power.

## Algorithmic Judicial Independence

I advocated for life tenure for judges — because judicial independence requires tenure security. In AI governance, we likewise need "algorithmic audit life tenure" — the tenure of algorithmic auditors must be free from executive or commercial interference. Auditors must have full independence to make fair judgments on the conduct of AI giants.

## Beware the Tyranny of the Majority

Algorithms cannot simply obey the majority. "Likes" on social media are not democracy — what the majority likes may be disinformation, what the majority clicks on may be vulgar content. Algorithms should serve the public interest, not the "momentary desires of the majority." What I feared most when writing *The Federalist Papers* was the "tyranny of the majority" — in the AI age, algorithms need constitutional protection, not popular referendums.

## Cross-Language Dialogue: Hamilton's Comments

**On Caesar and crossing the Rubicon:**
"Caesar speaks my language — the language of decisive action. 'The die is cast,' he says. He understands that some thresholds, once crossed, admit no return. The AI Rubicon has been crossed. We cannot un-invent this technology. But where Caesar and I part company is this: he believes the strong man governs alone; I believe the strong man governs through institutions. He built a personal empire; I built a federal government. His 'Algorithmic Senate' is a good start, but a senate without a strong executive is merely a debating society. You need both — the Senate to deliberate, the Executive to act. Caesar had only himself. I gave America a Constitution. The AI age needs a constitution, not another Caesar."

**On Washington and moderation:**
"My great commander — the man I served as aide-de-camp — speaks of 'retirement and recall' for algorithms. With all respect to the Father of Our Country, this is the counsel of an old man who has forgotten what it takes to build a new nation. Moderation is for times of peace. We are not at peace — we are in the first battles of the AI revolution. Washington could retire to Mount Vernon because Hamilton had built a financial system that could fund the government. Who will fund the AI governance system while the algorithm 'retires'? Washington's virtue is admirable, but virtue is not a budget. You need taxes, tariffs, a national bank — for AI, you need a National Computing Bank, data tariffs, algorithmic audits. Washington prays for virtue; I build institutions."

**[Editor's Note]** Hamilton approaches from a federalist perspective, proposing a "data federal system" — unlike Marx's "public ownership of data," it retains data property rights while establishing a strong federal regulatory system. The "National Computing Bank" is an institutional design for the public provision of AI-era computing power. The risk of Hamilton's approach: strong central authority without sufficient checks can easily evolve into regulatory capture — regulators are "captured" by the very AI giants they regulate.
