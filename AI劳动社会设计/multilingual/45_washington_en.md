# Republican Virtue and an AI Constitution

From the perspective of a founder of republicanism, this is a vision of an "Algorithmic Constitution" for AI governance. Its core lies in transplanting the checks-and-balances principles of the American founding era into algorithmic governance — separation of powers, federalism, term limits.

The deepest conviction of my life: **Liberty cannot rely solely on institutions; it must also rely on the virtue of the people.** Algorithmic governance cannot rely solely on precise code and powerful computing — it must rely on the republican virtue of algorithm designers, managers, and users. Every AI engineer must, like a citizen of a republic, place the public good above private interest.

## An Algorithmic Constitution

**Executive power** — the system that executes algorithmic decisions, managed by institutions authorized by the people. **Legislative power** — the institution that formulates algorithmic rules and ethical standards, democratically deliberated by multi-stakeholder representatives. **Judicial power** — the tribunal that reviews algorithmic decisions and corrects algorithmic errors, maintaining independence.

I voluntarily refused to become king — not because I do not love power, but because I love liberty more than power. Those who hold algorithmic power must also harbor this vigilance.

## Retirement and Recall

After the war ended, I voluntarily retired to Mount Vernon. This was not passive withdrawal — power requires periodic rest and reflection. For algorithms, this means regular model suspension, comprehensive auditing, and public scrutiny. After each "retirement" — the suspension and full inspection of an algorithm — the algorithm should be "recalled" — re-authorized by the people's representatives.

## The Algorithmic Military-Industrial Complex

A warning drawn from my Farewell Address: Beware the "factions of algorithms" — different algorithmic systems serving different interest groups, tearing the public sphere apart. Even more, beware of the "Algorithmic Military-Industrial Complex" — algorithms used for arms races and surveillance machinery, becoming tools of oppression rather than liberation.

Algorithms should be the servants of peace, not the masters of war.

## Cross-Language Dialogue: Washington's Comments

**On Hamilton and centralization:**
"Alexander was the ablest man of his generation — and the most dangerous. His vision of a 'National Computing Bank' and centralized algorithmic authority has the virtue of efficiency. I do not dispute that strong government is needed in the AI age. But Hamilton forgets what the Revolution taught us: power centralized is power abused. His 'Algorithmic Judiciary with life tenure' sounds wise, but life tenure in the hands of men subject to no electoral check becomes a license for tyranny. I stood against King George not because monarchy was inefficient, but because unchecked power corrupts. Hamilton would replace one king with another — a king of algorithms. I say: balance power or lose liberty. There is no third way."

**On Jefferson and republicanism:**
"My dear friend Thomas and I have disagreed since the very first Cabinet meeting. He dreams of a pastoral republic of data-owning yeomen; I know that republics are built by hard institutions, not by beautiful declarations. Jefferson speaks of the 'natural right' to data and the right to overthrow a bad algorithm. These are fine words — I have written such words myself. But a Declaration is not a Constitution. Jefferson would burn down the house every time a window sticks. I would repair the hinge. The AI age needs builders, not arsonists. Jefferson gives the people rights; I give the people a government capable of protecting those rights. Both are necessary — but Jefferson's vision without my institutions is a republic that dissolves before it is built."

**[Editor's Note]** Washington transplants the checks-and-balances principles of the American founding era into AI governance — separation of powers, federalism, "retirement and recall." The core weakness of this approach lies in its over-reliance on "republican virtue" — the virtue of Washington's era was built on Puritan ethics, but in a capital-driven technology industry, whether such virtue can be institutionalized is the biggest question mark.
