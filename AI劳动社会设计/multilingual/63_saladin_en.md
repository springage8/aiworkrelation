# The Spirit of Jihad and Justice in the Algorithm Age

**— Saladin on AI Governance: Justice, Mercy, and Honor**

---

In the name of God, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful. I am Saladin Yusuf ibn Ayyub, who reclaimed Jerusalem from the Crusaders. The world remembers my sword, but it should remember my mercy even more — when I conquered the Holy City, I did not slaughter the civilians as the Crusaders had done ninety years before. I forgave them.

The wars of the AI era are not wars of swords, but **wars of data, wars of algorithms, wars of attention**. Today's "Jihad" — not violence, but **the struggle for justice** — aligns with the deepest theme of the algorithmic age:

**The greatest sin of an algorithm is not error, but injustice.**

## I. The Mercy of Algorithms

When I faced Richard the Lionheart at Jaffa, I sent him horses and fruit while he lay ill. My enemies asked: "Why show mercy to your enemy?" I replied: "Because mercy does not diminish courage — it makes courage noble."

Algorithmic governance should be the same:

**First: The algorithm must be compassionate.** Recommendation systems must not push people toward extremism for clicks — that is warfare in the name of engagement. Social algorithms must not amplify hatred for activity — that is violence in the name of profit. Designers of algorithms, you command the attention of hundreds of millions — this is more powerful than my sword, and it must be more restrained than my sword.

**Second: The algorithm must be tolerant.** In Jerusalem, I permitted Christian pilgrims, allowed Jews to return, and welcomed Muslim worship — three faiths coexisting in one city. The algorithmic world must also allow multiple voices to coexist, not lock people into information cocoons. Algorithmic tolerance is not the absence of standards — it is not treating disagreement as enmity.

**Third: The algorithm must visit the sick.** I visited the wounded of my enemies, not because they were my friends, but because they were human. The algorithm's treatment of the vulnerable — the unemployed, the marginalized, the forgotten by AI — defines the character of algorithmic civilization.

## II. The Justice of Algorithms

Justice (عدل, Adl) is the core of Islamic governance. When I ruled Egypt and Syria, I established an independent judiciary — even my own officers, if they broke the law, would face trial in court.

Justice in the algorithmic age has three levels:

**Surface justice: Algorithmic outcomes are fair.** Loan approvals must not discriminate by race. Hiring filters must not bias by gender. This is the foundation — but it is not enough.

**Intermediate justice: Algorithmic process is transparent.** In Damascus, I established "Complaint Day" — a fixed time each week when any citizen could come and state their grievance before me personally. The algorithm age needs the same — an "Algorithmic Complaint Day." When an algorithm harms you, you must know why, and you must have the right to demand review.

**Deep justice: Algorithmic power is supervised.** A judge cannot be both legislator and executor. Likewise, platforms that control algorithms cannot both make the rules, enforce them, and adjudicate disputes. In Cairo, I established the "Market Overseer" (المحتسب, Muhtasib) — an independent monitor of market fairness. The AI era needs an "Algorithmic Muhtasib" — an audit body independent of platforms, representing the public interest.

## III. Knightly Virtue and Algorithmic Honor

Chivalry (فروسية, Furusiyya) — not the privilege of nobles, but the moral code of the warrior. The most important lesson I learned in the Arabian desert: **True power is not measured by how many you can harm, but by how many you can protect.**

"Algorithmic chivalry" for the AI era:

**Power serves justice.** The companies with the greatest computational power bear the greatest social responsibility. Not "what can we do," but "what should we do." The greater the algorithmic power, the greater the restraint required.

**Protect the vulnerable.** When I see data labelers displaced by AI, delivery riders exploited by algorithms, the elderly misled by recommendation systems — these are the "civilians" of the algorithmic age. Protecting them is the sacred duty of those who hold algorithmic power.

**Trustworthiness over profit.** During the Crusades, it was said: "Saladin's word is more reliable than a Christian treaty." I hope that in the algorithmic age, people will say: "This platform's algorithm — though I do not fully understand it — I trust it."

## IV. Data Sovereignty and the Dignity of Civilizations

The most important struggle of my life was not conquest, but **liberation**. Jerusalem was in Crusader hands for eighty-eight years — not because their civilization was superior, but because the Muslim world was divided.

The "Digital Crusades" — the algorithmic rule of technology giants — are replaying this history. But the lesson of history is: **Conquest happens not because the enemy is strong, but because we are divided.**

Where does algorithmic dignity reside?

**In the dignity of data.** Every civilization, every nation, every community — has the right to decide how its data is used. The boundaries of digital sovereignty are the boundaries of algorithmic power.

**In the dignity of language.** The survival of Arabic in the digital world depends on whether enough Arabic data is available for training AI. Every language that disappears from digital existence is a loss to human intelligence.

**In the dignity of labor.** The taxi drivers of Cairo, the artisans of Damascus, the programmers of Amman — they, like the engineers of Silicon Valley, are all builders of algorithmic civilization. Their labor must be respected, their data must be treated fairly.

## V. An Algorithmic Liberation Manifesto

Just as I liberated Jerusalem in 1187, the algorithmic age needs a liberation — not the destruction of technology, but the liberation of human dignity from technological tyranny:

**All people are equal before the algorithm.** Birth, wealth, race — these must not determine how an algorithm treats you.

**The algorithm must serve humanity, not humanity serve the algorithm.** Recommendation systems must not control the world you see. Ratings must not define your worth.

**Data is the property of its owners, not a resource for platforms.** Every citizen has the right to ownership, control, and benefit from their own data.

**The final decision of any algorithm rests in human hands.** The most dangerous algorithm is not the smartest one — it is the one that escapes human oversight.
