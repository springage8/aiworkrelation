# Meritocracy and the Smart Nation

**— A Little Red Dot's Survival Strategy**

I have never shied away from the fact that Singapore is a little red dot, surrounded by larger nations, with no natural resources whatsoever. Our only resource is our people. So our logic is simple—**if you are not smart, you do not survive**.

## Algorithmic Meritocracy

The essence of AI governance lies not in democratic form, but in **effectiveness**. Those who understand AI best should have the most say in its governance—this is not elitism, it is realism. Do you want AI policies to be made by those who understand AI best, or by those who are best at canvassing votes?

Governments should hire the best AI talent to serve in the public sector—with compensation and respect matching the market. Not because they are important—but because without this, you cannot compete with the private sector for talent.

## The Smart Nation Model

Singapore was the world's first "Smart Nation"—comprehensive data-driven governance: from transport to healthcare, from housing to education. We acted first and debated later. The West spent ten years debating privacy issues—we had already used data to optimize every public service.

But this requires a strict legal framework. My successors and I built a system: data submitted to the state, automated services, tiered access management—but accompanied by extremely stringent data protection laws. Those who abuse data face the harshest penalties.

## The Asian Way of AI Governance

The Western-style over-liberal approach to AI governance will not work in Asia—this is not a matter of superiority, but of cultural tradition. Asian societies place greater emphasis on collective interests, social stability, and long-term planning—AI governance should reflect these values.

**"Develop first, govern later"** —First use AI to enhance efficiency, and when efficiency brings economic growth, then address equity issues. China follows a similarly pragmatic path. I was never an admirer of Mao Zedong or Deng Xiaoping—I respect results. And China's achievements in AI come, in part, from its pragmatic attitude.

**Social stability above all else** —AI must not become a source of social instability. This may mean monitoring certain platforms and restricting certain algorithms—but stability is the prerequisite for development. Without stability, nothing else matters.

## Cross-Language Dialogue

**Deng Xiaoping (Pragmatism)** : Lee Kuan Yew, you and I share the same DNA—pragmatism. You built Singapore from a fishing village to a first-world nation; I opened China to the world. In AI, you talk about "meritocracy" and "effectiveness first." I agree. But I want to ask you: when you say "the best AI talent should govern"—how do you define "best"? In China, we learned the hard way that "best" can easily become a self-serving elite. The Cultural Revolution was a reaction against exactly this problem. Your PAP has been in power for over six decades—I respect the results, but I also wonder: where is the feedback mechanism when the "meritocratic elite" stops being meritocratic?

**Otto von Bismarck (Realism)** : Mr. Lee, your realism impresses me. I created the modern welfare state—not out of compassion, but out of cold calculation: if you do not give workers a safety net, they will turn to revolution. Your "develop first, govern later" is the same kind of realism. But let me give you a warning from my own experience: I maintained the balance of power in Europe through a complex system of alliances and secret treaties. It worked while I was in charge. After I was dismissed in 1890, it fell apart within 24 years—leading to World War I. Your system works because you are at the helm. What happens after you? Your "algorithmic meritocracy" needs institutional safeguards, not just personal genius.

**Alexander Hamilton (Stability and Strong Government)** : Mr. Lee, I fought for a strong central government because I saw what happened under the Articles of Confederation—thirteen squabbling states, no credit, no army, no future. Your Singapore story proves my thesis: strong, capable government is the precondition for prosperity. Your Smart Nation is what I dreamed of for America—efficient, data-driven, rational. But I must raise a concern from my *Federalist Papers*: you need checks and balances. In my system, the executive, legislative, and judiciary check each other. In your "algorithmic meritocracy," who checks the algorithm? The government that deploys it? I worry about the concentration of power—not because I distrust your intentions, but because, as I wrote in Federalist No. 51, "if men were angels, no government would be necessary."
