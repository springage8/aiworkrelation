## Foreword: The Policy Template Is Not Theory, It Is a Weapon

Theory must be transformed into policy in order to change reality. The purpose of this template is:

1. To provide lawmakers who support "data common ownership" and "algorithmic democracy" in various countries with a ready-made proposal framework
2. To ensure that policy proposals advanced around the world share a unified core philosophy and comparable specific provisions
3. To enable civil society to use this template to pressure governments -- "Other countries are already discussing this plan"

**Instructions for use:**
- Each policy area is divided into a "Basic Version" (easier to pass) and an "Advanced Version" (requires stronger political will)
- Proponents should select the appropriate entry point based on their country's political environment
- Content in \[brackets\] should be adjusted according to the specific country's legal system

## Chapter One: Data Rights Bill

### 1.1 Basic Version: Data Rights Charter

**Policy goal:** Establish citizens' fundamental rights over their personal data

**Recommended provisions:**

**Article 1: Principle of Data Sovereignty**
> The personal data of \[Country Name\]'s citizens belongs to the citizens who generate it. Any entity that collects, stores, or processes personal data within the territory of \[Country Name\], regardless of its place of registration, is subject to this law.

**Article 2: Right to Informed Consent**
> Data collectors must inform data subjects in a clear, explicit, and understandable manner of: (1) what data is collected, (2) for what purpose, (3) who will access this data, and (4) the data retention period. Implied consent does not constitute valid consent.

**Article 3: Right to Data Portability**
> Data subjects have the right to obtain their data in a structured, commonly used, machine-readable format and have the right to transmit that data directly to other service providers.

**Article 4: Right to Data Benefits**
> When data is used for commercial purposes, data subjects have the right to fair compensation. Compensation may take the form of monetary payment or equivalent services or other benefits.

**Article 5: Right to Erasure (Right to be Forgotten)**
> Data subjects have the right to request deletion of their personal data, unless otherwise provided by law.

**Legislative basis:**
- The GDPR (EU) has already established precedent for similar rights
- Brazil's LGPD and India's Digital Personal Data Protection Act have localized practices
- These provisions do not subvert the existing economic order and are easily accepted

### 1.2 Advanced Version: Data Public Interest Law

**Policy goal:** Recognize data as having the attributes of a public good and establish a data public interest system

**Recommended provisions:**

**Article 1: Dual Nature of Data**
> Data possesses both the attributes of personal property and those of a social public good. When the aggregation of personal data reaches a certain scale, its public good attributes take precedence over its personal property attributes.

**Article 2: Data Public Trust System**
> Establish the \[Country Name\] Data Public Trust as an independent institution for managing public data. The composition of the Trust Board shall include data subject representatives, technical experts, academic institutions, and government representatives, with data subject representatives comprising no less than one-third.

**Article 3: Mandatory Data Public Trust Custody**
> Data must be placed in the custody of the Data Public Trust in the following circumstances: (1) data involving national security or public health; (2) user behavioral data from platforms reaching \[X\] users; (3) research data collected with public funds.

**Article 4: Public Distribution of Data Dividends**
> The operating income of the Data Public Trust, after deducting operating costs, shall be used for the following public purposes: (1) digital literacy education, (2) digitalization of public services, (3) AI workers' protection fund.

## Chapter Two: Algorithmic Governance Bill

### 2.1 Basic Version: Algorithmic Transparency Law

**Policy goal:** Require algorithmic decision-making processes to be transparent to those affected

**Recommended provisions:**

**Article 1: Algorithmic Impact Assessment**
> Any entity that uses algorithms to make decisions having a significant impact on individuals must conduct an annual algorithmic impact assessment and publish the results. Significant impacts include, but are not limited to: employment opportunities, credit access, insurance pricing, service access, and pricing strategies.

**Article 2: Obligation of Algorithmic Explainability**
> When an algorithm makes an adverse decision regarding an individual, the affected person has the right to receive a clear explanation of that decision. The explanation must be provided in plain, understandable language and include the main factors in the decision and their weightings.

**Article 3: Right to Object and Appeal Algorithmic Decisions**
> When an individual objects to an algorithmic decision, they have the right to request a human review. The reviewer must have no conflict of interest with the designer or operator of the algorithm.

**Article 4: Algorithmic Discrimination Testing**
> Algorithm operators must regularly test and publish the discriminatory impact of their algorithms, including but not limited to differential impacts related to race, gender, age, and geographic region.

**Legislative basis:**
- The EU AI Act already requires transparency and explainability for high-risk AI systems
- New York City Local Law 144 already requires auditing of algorithmic hiring tools
- These provisions mainly target "algorithmic opacity" and do not touch the system of algorithmic ownership

### 2.2 Advanced Version: Algorithmic Democracy Law

**Policy goal:** Establish the democratic right of affected persons to participate in algorithmic management

**Recommended provisions:**

**Article 1: Algorithmic Management Committee**
> Any entity that uses algorithms to manage more than \[X\] workers must establish an Algorithmic Management Committee. The committee shall be composed equally of worker representatives and management representatives, and shall be responsible for overseeing the design and modification of algorithms.

**Article 2: Right to Negotiate Algorithmic Changes**
> Algorithm operators may not unilaterally modify algorithms that have a significant impact on workers' income or working conditions. Any such modification must obtain the consent of the Algorithmic Management Committee through consultation.

**Article 3: Independence of Algorithmic Audits**
> Algorithm audits must be conducted by independent third parties. Audit reports shall be published to all affected workers and the public.

## Chapter Three: Digital Labor Protection Bill

### 3.1 Basic Version: Platform Workers Rights Law

**Policy goal:** Bring platform workers within the scope of basic labor protections

**Recommended provisions:**

**Article 1: Reclassification of Platform Workers**
> Platform workers who meet any of the following conditions shall be considered legal "employees" rather than "independent contractors": (1) the platform sets the price for services, (2) the platform assigns tasks rather than workers choosing independently, (3) the platform unilaterally changes working conditions, (4) the worker's income depends on the platform for more than \[X%\].

**Article 2: Minimum Income Guarantee**
> The net income of platform workers (after deducting platform fees) shall not be less than \[X%\] of the statutory minimum wage. (Recommendation: 120%, to cover tool and transportation costs)

**Article 3: Social Security Coverage**
> Platforms must contribute to social insurance for workers (health insurance, pension insurance, workers' compensation insurance). Premiums shall be calculated at \[X%\] of the worker's platform income.

**Article 4: Working Hours Limits**
> Platforms may not, through algorithms or other means, force workers to work continuously for more than \[X\] hours. Platforms must implement mandatory rest mechanisms.

**Legislative basis:**
- Spain's "Rider Law" (Ley Rider) has established the employee status of platform workers
- California's AB5 established the ABC test standard
- The UK Supreme Court's Uber case confirmed drivers as "workers"

### 3.2 Advanced Version: AI Transition Assistance Law

**Policy goal:** Provide transition support for workers displaced by AI

**Recommended provisions:**

**Article 1: Advance Notice Obligation for AI Replacement**
> Enterprises planning to use AI to replace human labor on a large scale must notify relevant government departments and affected workers \[X\] months in advance and submit a "Replacement Impact Assessment Report."

**Article 2: AI Replacement Tax**
> Enterprises that save on labor costs by using AI to replace human labor shall pay an "AI Replacement Tax" at a rate of \[X%\] of the savings, to fund retraining and transition support for displaced workers.

**Article 3: Universal Basic Digital Dividend**
> Using funds drawn from Data Public Trust revenues and AI replacement tax revenues, a "Digital Dividend" shall be distributed annually to all citizens. The per-person annual amount shall be no less than \[X\].

**Article 4: Reduce Working Hours Without Reducing Pay**
> In sectors where AI has significantly increased productivity, promote industry-wide collective bargaining to gradually reduce the standard work week (from 40 hours to 32 hours and ultimately to 24 hours) while maintaining wage levels.

## Chapter Four: Digital Economy Transformation Bill

### 4.1 Basic Version: Digital Cooperative Promotion Law

**Policy goal:** Support the establishment and development of data cooperatives and platform cooperatives

**Recommended provisions:**

**Article 1: Legal Status of Digital Cooperatives**
> Clarify the legal status of data cooperatives and platform cooperatives as legitimate forms of economic organization, allowing them to register and enjoy the same legal rights as traditional cooperatives.

**Article 2: Support Policies for Digital Cooperatives**
> (1) Tax incentives: the first \[X\] years of cooperative income shall be exempt from corporate income tax; (2) Financing support: establish a "Digital Cooperative Development Fund" to provide low-interest loans; (3) Technical support: the government shall provide free cooperative management training and technical services; (4) Government procurement preference: the government shall prioritize procurement of cooperative services.

**Article 3: Collective Ownership of Data**
> Members of a data cooperative shall have collective ownership of data collected by the cooperative, and internal cooperative decision-making shall follow the "one person, one vote" principle.

### 4.2 Advanced Version: Public Digital Infrastructure Construction Law

**Policy goal:** Bring key digital infrastructure under public ownership

**Recommended provisions:**

**Article 1: Public Cloud Computing Service**
> Establish the \[Country Name\] Public Cloud Computing Platform, providing free or cost-price cloud computing services to domestic enterprises, government agencies, and research institutions.

**Article 2: Public Data Spaces**
> Establish public data spaces covering key sectors (healthcare, education, agriculture, transportation) to promote data sharing and utilization in these areas.

**Article 3: Public Algorithm Library**
> Establish a public algorithm library, open-sourcing and sharing algorithms used for public services, reducing dependence on commercial algorithms.

## Chapter Five: International Governance Initiatives

### 5.1 Basic Version: Cross-Border Data Flow Agreement

**Policy goal:** Establish fair rules for cross-border data flows

**Recommended provisions:**

**Article 1: Data Localization Storage**
> Important data (as defined in the annex) collected in \[Country Name\] must be stored within the country. Transfer of data abroad must undergo assessment and approval by the Data Public Trust.

**Article 2: Principle of Reciprocal Access**
> When other countries request that \[Country Name\] provide data, \[Country Name\] has the right to demand reciprocal data access rights.

### 5.2 Advanced Version: Global Algorithmic Governance Convention

**Policy goal:** Promote the establishment of an international algorithmic governance framework

**Recommended provisions:**

**Article 1: Global Algorithmic Governance Organization**
> Promote the establishment of a "Global Algorithmic Governance Organization" (GAO) under the framework of the United Nations, responsible for formulating and coordinating global algorithmic governance standards.

**Article 2: Global Data Rights Convention**
> Promote the formulation of a "Global Data Rights Convention," establishing a global consensus on basic principles such as data sovereignty, data justice, and data as a public good.

**Article 3: International Protection of Digital Workers' Rights**
> Within the framework of the International Labour Organization (ILO), promote the adoption of a "Digital Workers Rights Convention," setting minimum protection standards for digital workers worldwide.

## Chapter Six: Legislative Strategy

### 6.1 What Constitutes a Good "First Step" Policy?

**Selection criteria:**
1. **High political feasibility** -- does not touch the most powerful vested interests
2. **Strong social consensus** -- easily understood and supported by the public
3. **Precedent exists** -- similar laws already passed in other countries
4. **Produces tangible benefits** -- directly affects people's lives once passed

**Recommended first-wave policy list (in order of priority):**

| No. | Policy | Rationale |
|-----|--------|-----------|
| 1 | Algorithmic Transparency Law (Basic) | EU AI Act precedent; tech companies can hardly publicly oppose "transparency" |
| 2 | Data Rights Charter (Basic) | GDPR has proven feasibility; minimal economic disruption |
| 3 | Platform Workers Minimum Income Guarantee | Spain's Rider Law precedent; strong sense of social fairness |
| 4 | Digital Cooperative Promotion Law | Does not disturb large companies; supports "small beating big" |

### 6.2 Bill Drafting Suggestions

1. **Keep it concise** -- 30-50 articles, so legislators and the public can read it through
2. **Definitions must be precise** -- key terms such as "algorithm," "data subject," "platform worker" must be clearly defined
3. **Penalties must be clear** -- the amount and form of penalties for violations
4. **Transition period must be reasonable** -- give enterprises and government 6-18 months to adapt
5. **Evaluation mechanism must be built-in** -- effectiveness must be assessed 2-3 years after the law takes effect
