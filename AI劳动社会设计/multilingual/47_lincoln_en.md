# AI of the People, by the People, for the People

"A house divided against itself cannot stand." — A society where AI benefits are unequally distributed will inevitably collapse.

## Gettysburg

Four score and seven years ago, our fathers brought forth on this continent a new nation — conceived in liberty, and dedicated to the proposition that all men are created equal.

Four score and seven years later, humanity stands at the door of the AI age. The government of the algorithm age — AI — must be **of the people** (belonging to the people, not to a few capital giants), **by the people** (governed by the people, not monopolized by a technological elite), **for the people** (serving the people, not maximizing shareholder returns).

## The Algorithmic Emancipation Proclamation

On January 1, 1863, I signed the Emancipation Proclamation — declaring that those who were enslaved should thenceforth be free.

Today, in the same spirit, I sign the "Algorithmic Emancipation Proclamation" — declaring data workers liberated from algorithmic slavery. Data annotators are no longer invisible digital laborers, but workers entitled to dignity. Workers displaced by AI are not discarded failures, but a transformation cost to be borne by society as a whole.

## The Malice of the Second Inaugural

In my Second Inaugural Address, I said: "With malice toward none, with charity for all."

Algorithms too must allow people room to err. Your credit score should not be permanently affected by a single mistake. Your social account should not be permanently banned because of one ill-considered expression. In an algorithmic society, **the possibility of redemption** is part of human dignity.

## AI for the People

"For the people" — AI serves the people, not the people serving AI. The productivity gains from AI must be transformed into benefits shared by all: shorter working hours, better healthcare, richer educational resources. If AI advances but people's lives become more anxious — this AI is not "for the people," it is "for the people's worry."

## Cross-Language Dialogue: Lincoln's Comments

**On Jefferson and equality:**
"Thomas Jefferson wrote the words that have guided this nation: 'All men are created equal.' He was a man of profound vision — but also of profound contradiction. He penned those words while owning slaves. In the AI age, many who speak of 'data rights' and 'algorithmic freedom' are themselves building systems that concentrate power and exclude the powerless. Mr. Jefferson speaks of the right to overthrow a bad algorithm government — and I agree. But I learned in 1861 that secession is not the same as liberation. Breaking apart a bad system is easy; building a better one is hard. Jefferson gives the people a Declaration; I give them a Union. Both are needed — but the Declaration without the Union is just a piece of paper, as we learned in the long years of civil war. The AI age needs not just declarations of rights, but a union of purpose strong enough to enforce them."

**On Roosevelt and freedom:**
"Franklin Roosevelt understood something that took me four years of civil war to learn: that freedom is not free. His Four Freedoms — especially 'freedom from want' — recognize that liberty without material security is a cruel joke. A man who is hungry is not free to choose. A worker displaced by AI who has no safety net is not free to refuse exploitation. My Emancipation Proclamation freed the slaves in law; Roosevelt's New Deal sought to free working people from economic desperation. In the AI age, we need both: the legal emancipation from algorithmic bondage, and the economic security that makes that emancipation real. Roosevelt and I — we are brothers across time. He fought the Great Depression; I fought the great division. The AI age fights both at once."

---

**[Editor's Note]** Lincoln injects the core declaration of American politics — "of the people, by the people, for the people" — into the AI governance debate, endowing AI with a moral mission. The "Algorithmic Emancipation Proclamation" elevates the rights of data workers to the historical level of the abolition of slavery. The strength of Lincoln's approach lies in its moral appeal, while its weakness lies in the lack of specific institutional design — how "for the people" is to be realized is not a natural institutional evolution, but requires complex interest negotiation and institutional innovation.
