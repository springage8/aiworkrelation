# Southeast Asia Digital Workers Alliance Plan

## — Cross-Border Platform Worker Solidarity —

**Author: Mao Zedong** | *Written May 2026*

*English translation of the Chinese original by Mao Zedong*

---

## Foreword: The Particularity of Southeast Asia

Southeast Asia is the "testing ground" of the global digital platform economy:

- **Birthplace of super-platforms** -- Grab (valued at $40 billion+) and Gojek started here, covering ride-hailing, food delivery, payments, and e-commerce
- **The world's largest number of riders** -- Over **5 million** riders and drivers in Southeast Asia depend on the platform economy for their livelihood
- **Second hub for data annotation** -- The Philippines and Indonesia are another global center for AI data annotation and content moderation
- **Arena of US-China tech competition** -- Southeast Asia is the focal point of competition between US and Chinese tech capital; this competition creates special space for labor organizing

The special contradiction of Southeast Asia lies in:

> **Platforms are the hope for Southeast Asian countries' digital economic development, but the algorithmic management of platforms is creating new relations of exploitation. The people of Southeast Asia want the dividends of the digital economy, but do not want to be controlled by algorithms.**

Seizing this contradiction is the key to unlocking Southeast Asia.

## Chapter One: Class Analysis of Southeast Asian Platforms

### 1.1 Riders and Drivers (Platform Proletariat)

**Distribution:** Indonesia (Jakarta, Surabaya, Bandung), Malaysia (Kuala Lumpur), Singapore, Thailand (Bangkok), Vietnam (Ho Chi Minh City, Hanoi), Philippines (Manila)

**Platforms:** Grab, Gojek, ShopeeFood, Lalamove, Maxim (Russian platform expanding in Vietnam)

**Typical Income:**
- Indonesia: $200-400/month (above minimum wage but below living wage)
- Philippines: $150-300/month (below minimum wage)
- Singapore: $1,000-1,500/month (relatively higher but cost of living is much higher)

**Exploitation Mechanisms:**
- Platform commission rates as high as 20-30%
- Opaque algorithmic order allocation (riders do not know how orders are distributed)
- Dynamic pricing (surge pricing revenue goes to the platform, not the rider)
- False "flexible employment" -- in reality, riders are locked into specific time slots by algorithms

**Existing Organizing Attempts:**
- Indonesia: Gojek Riders Alliance (founded 2020) and Grab Riders Alliance
- Philippines: FDSP (Food Delivery Service Partners Alliance)
- Malaysia: Malaysian Gig Economy Workers Union

**Organizing Strategy:**
- From WhatsApp/Telegram groups to formal organization -- utilize Southeast Asia's high-penetration communication apps
- Make "algorithmic transparency" the core demand -- a pain point shared by all riders
- Collaborate with university researchers to quantify the extent of algorithmic exploitation

### 1.2 Content Moderators and Data Annotators

**Distribution:** Philippines (Manila, Cebu), Indonesia (Jakarta)

**Background:**
- The Philippines is an English-speaking country and the world's second-largest center for AI content moderation and data annotation (after Kenya)
- Facebook, Google, and TikTok have extensive content moderation outsourcing in the Philippines
- Hourly wage $2-5/hour (far below US counterparts at $15-20/hour)

**Special Challenges:**
- Extremely strict work confidentiality agreements
- Severe mental health problems that are covered up
- Data annotators are not considered "formal workers"

**Organizing Strategy:**
- Cooperate with the Philippine Department of Labor and Employment to push for "digital worker" classification reform
- Utilize the Philippines' overseas worker network (many Filipinos work as content moderators abroad)
- Push for legislation requiring platforms to disclose content moderation working conditions and mental health protections

### 1.3 Small Merchants on E-commerce and Social Commerce

**Distribution:** All of Southeast Asia

**Background:**
- Tens of millions of small merchants on Shopee, Lazada, TikTok Shop
- These small merchants depend on platforms for traffic but are controlled by platform algorithms
- Platform commissions + advertising fees squeeze profit margins

**Class Position:**
Small merchants are not the proletariat, but the petty bourgeoisie. In revolution, the petty bourgeoisie is vacillating -- they support change when it benefits them, but may oppose it when it touches their interests.

**Organizing Strategy:**
- Do not use them as the main force, but they can be part of the "united front"
- Push for "platform data openness" -- allow small merchants to access their own data
- Push for "fair commissions" -- reduce platform commission rates

## Chapter Two: Southeast Asia's Unique Contradictions and Opportunities

### 2.1 Principal Contradiction: US-China Tech Competition vs. ASEAN Autonomy

Southeast Asia is the frontline of US-China tech competition:
- **US camp:** Google, Meta, Amazon have significant investments in Southeast Asia
- **China camp:** Grab (Tencent investment), Gojek (ByteDance, Tencent investment), Shopee (Sea Group), TikTok Shop
- **ASEAN autonomy:** ASEAN countries want to maintain balance between the two camps

**Strategic Significance:**

US-China competition creates special space for labor organizing:

1. **Space A (toward US capital):** "You say you want democracy and freedom -- then why are your AI platforms exploiting Southeast Asian workers?"
2. **Space B (toward Chinese capital):** "You say you want common prosperity -- then why are your algorithms squeezing Southeast Asian riders?"
3. **Space C (toward ASEAN governments):** "We can help you build your own digital sovereignty between China and the US"

**Develop in the cracks between the two camps -- this is Southeast Asia's strategic value.**

### 2.2 Special Opportunity: ASEAN's Policy Framework

ASEAN has the following policy levers that can be utilized:

| Mechanism | Use |
|-----------|-----|
| ASEAN Digital Integration Framework | Can promote "digital worker rights" as an ASEAN agenda at the regional level |
| ASEAN Labor Ministers Meeting | Convened annually; platform worker rights can be raised as a topic |
| ASEAN Human Rights Declaration | Can invoke the "right to decent work" to claim rights |
| ASEAN-China FTA Upgrade | Include labor clauses in trade agreements |

### 2.3 Special Challenge: Language and National Border Fragmentation

Southeast Asia's biggest challenge: **11 countries, 10 major languages, different political systems.**

Countermeasures:
- **Multilingual organizing materials** -- declarations and action guides need to be translated into local languages
- **"Virtual alliance" rather than "unified organization"** -- each country maintains autonomy, coordinating actions through the alliance
- **Focus on common pain points** -- cross-border common goals (algorithmic transparency, data rights, fair commissions)

## Chapter Three: Three-Phase Action Roadmap

### 3.1 Phase One: Taking Root at the National Level (2026-2028)

**Indonesia (Main Battlefield):**
- Goal: Transform Grab and Gojek rider alliances from loose groups into formal unions
- Tactic: Launch "Algorithmic Transparency Campaign" -- demand platforms disclose order allocation logic
- Legal pathway: Push the Constitutional Court to confirm that "algorithmic management" constitutes an employment relationship

**Philippines (Second Battlefield):**
- Goal: Establish a nationwide "Digital Workers Alliance"
- Tactic: Use content moderators' mental health as a breakthrough point (high social sympathy)
- Legal pathway: Push for "Digital Workers Protection Law" legislation

**Thailand/Vietnam/Malaysia (Expansion Battlefields):**
- Collect rider data, quantify the extent of exploitation
- Build preliminary Telegram/WhatsApp group networks
- Establish cooperative relationships with local scholars and NGOs

### 3.2 Phase Two: Regional Alliance Formation (2028-2032)

**Specific Tasks:**
1. Convene the first "Southeast Asia Digital Workers Congress" (rotating host countries)
2. Establish the permanent secretariat of the "Southeast Asia Digital Workers Alliance" (SEDWA)
3. Launch the "Southeast Asia Algorithmic Transparency Charter" campaign
4. Establish a regional legal aid fund

**Key Indicators:**
- Alliance covers 50,000+ riders
- Successfully coordinate 2 or more cross-border actions (e.g., simultaneous strikes)
- Algorithmic transparency campaign achieves legislative progress in at least 2 countries

### 3.3 Phase Three: Structural Transformation (2032-2040)

**Specific Tasks:**
1. Push ASEAN to pass a "Digital Workers Rights Framework"
2. Establish "data cooperatives" -- riders collectively own data and bargain collectively with platforms
3. Promote the establishment of a Southeast Asia Data Trust (regional data storage and processing)

**Key Indicators:**
- ASEAN framework passed
- At least 1 data cooperative successfully operating
- Southeast Asia becomes a model for global platform worker organizing

## Chapter Four: Specific Campaign Plans

### 4.1 The "Algorithmic Transparency" Campaign

This is the first major battle of the entire Southeast Asian digital labor movement.

**Why choose this issue:**
- All riders care about this issue (regardless of country or platform)
- Platforms find it hard to refute ("Why can't you tell workers how orders are allocated?")
- Technical feasibility exists (algorithm auditing tools can be developed)

**Campaign Phases:**
1. **Exposure Phase (Months 1-6):** Collect rider reports, systematically document cases of algorithmic opacity
2. **Mobilization Phase (Months 6-12):** Publish "Algorithm Black Box" report, hold public discussions
3. **Pressure Phase (Months 12-24):** Launch open letters, petitions, small-scale work stoppages
4. **Legislation Phase (Months 24-36):** Push for legislation requiring algorithmic transparency

### 4.2 The "Fair Commission" Campaign

**Goal:** Reduce platform commission rates from 20-30% to 10-15%

**Special Strategy:**
- Exploit competition between platforms -- Grab, Gojek, ShopeeFood compete with each other
- "Any platform willing to lower commissions, we will recommend to riders for priority use" -- divide the platforms

### 4.3 The "One-Day Alliance" Campaign

**Goal:** Organize simultaneous work stoppages in different countries

**Method:**
- Choose a date (e.g., May Day / International Workers' Day)
- Organize simultaneous rider work stoppages in Indonesia, Philippines, Thailand, and Malaysia
- Common demands: algorithmic transparency + fair commissions + data rights

**Significance:** Demonstrate the capacity for cross-border solidarity, exert real pressure on platforms

## Chapter Five: Risks and Countermeasures

| Risk | Probability | Countermeasure |
|------|------------|---------------|
| Platforms dismantle the movement through divide-and-conquer tactics | High | Establish a unified "minimum program" to prevent being picked off one by one |
| Government repression (especially in Thailand, Cambodia, Myanmar) | Medium-High | Start in countries with greater democratic space (Indonesia, Philippines) |
| Organizers being co-opted | Medium | Rotating leadership, financial transparency |
| Workers from different countries being pitted against each other by platforms | Medium | Emphasize "the common enemy is the algorithm, not workers of other countries" |
| Cross-border coordination costs too high | Medium | Primarily virtual organization, reduce offline coordination costs |

---

## Conclusion

The strategic significance of the Southeast Asian digital workers' movement lies in:

1. **It is the "birthplace" of the global platform economy** -- The Grab and Gojek models are being replicated worldwide. If workers' power can be built in the birthplace, it will have a demonstration effect globally.
2. **It is a "crack" in US-China tech competition** -- Competition between the two camps creates special negotiating space for workers.
3. **It is a natural laboratory for the "united front"** -- A multi-ethnic, multi-lingual, multi-religious, multi-political system environment forces the movement to learn how to build the broadest possible unity.

"A single spark can start a prairie fire." -- The spark of Southeast Asia, if used well, can spread to every corner of the global platform economy.
