# South Asia Data Annotators Rights Movement Plan

## — The First Cry from the Lowest Rung of the AI Industry Chain —

**Author: Mao Zedong** | *Written May 2026*

*English translation of the Chinese original by Mao Zedong*

---

## Foreword: Who Are the "Indentured Laborers" of the AI Era?

In the AI industry chain, data annotation is the most basic, most labor-intensive link. And South Asia -- India, Pakistan, Bangladesh, Sri Lanka, Nepal -- is the global center of data annotation.

If Kenya is the capital of content moderation, **South Asia is the capital of data annotation.**

The digital workers here are providing the most basic "nourishment" for the operation of global AI -- hundreds of millions of annotated data points -- while receiving a pittance in return.

This is not a "deviation that needs correction"; this is the inevitable result of capitalism in the global division of labor. The "primitive accumulation" described by Marx in *Capital* is being reenacted in South Asia in digital form. The difference is that what was accumulated in the 19th century was land and machinery; what is accumulated in the 21st century is data and the surplus value of annotation labor.

## Chapter One: Class Analysis of the South Asian Data Annotation Industry

### 1.1 Industry Panorama

The scale of the South Asian data annotation industry:

| Country | Workers (Estimated) | Typical Hourly Wage | Main Business Types |
|---------|-------------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| India | 500,000-1,000,000 | $0.50-2.00 | Image annotation, speech transcription, NLU training |
| Pakistan | 100,000-200,000 | $0.50-1.50 | Speech transcription (Urdu), content moderation |
| Bangladesh | 50,000-100,000 | $0.30-1.00 | Image annotation, basic data entry |
| Sri Lanka | 20,000-50,000 | $0.80-2.00 | NLP annotation, sentiment analysis |
| Nepal | 10,000-30,000 | $0.40-1.20 | Image annotation, data entry |

**Major Annotation Platforms:** Amazon Mechanical Turk, Appen, Lionbridge, iMerit, CrowdFlower (now Figure Eight), in-house outsourcing companies

### 1.2 Indian Data Annotators -- Vanguard of the South Asian Movement

**Distribution:**
- Bangalore, Hyderabad, Chennai (large city outsourcing centers)
- Rural areas (remote work through digital platforms)

**Nature of Work:**
- Annotating road images for autonomous driving companies
- Annotating product images for e-commerce platforms
- Annotating harmful content for social media
- Transcribing audio for voice assistants

**Exploitation Characteristics:**
- Piece-rate wages: $0.01-0.05 per image, $0.02-0.10 per transcription
- No labor contract, no social security, no health insurance
- "Training period" (1-2 weeks) with no pay or extremely low pay
- Platforms unilaterally modify pricing rules

### 1.3 Special Predicaments of South Asian Data Annotators

**Predicament One: Global Competitive Price Suppression**

Annotators in South Asia not only compete with workers in their own country but also with annotators in the Philippines, Kenya, Venezuela, and other countries. Platforms use this global "race to the bottom" to continuously drive down prices.

**Predicament Two: Linguistic "Super-Exploitation"**

South Asia's multilingual capacity should be an advantage, but:
- Annotation fees for low-resource languages are extremely low (Urdu, Bengali, Tamil, etc.)
- Annotators also need to annotate in English -- English proficiency is treated as a "basic requirement" with no premium paid
- Linguistic expertise is not recognized

**Predicament Three: Middleman Exploitation**

The data annotation industry chain:
```
Tech Giants (OpenAI/Google) → Annotation Outsourcing Platforms (Appen/iMerit)
→ Local Intermediary Companies → Team Leaders → Annotators
```
Each layer extracts profit, leaving annotators with less than 10% of the final contract price.

### 1.4 Class Position: Outpost of the Digital Proletariat

South Asian data annotators are the most representative group of the digital proletariat in the AI era:

- **They directly serve AI production** -- without their annotations, there would be no AI models
- **Their labor is extremely devalued** -- at the very bottom of the global value chain
- **They are being replaced by AI itself** -- synthetic data, active learning, and semi-supervised learning are reducing the demand for manual annotation

This last characteristic is the most critical: **Capital is extracting their surplus value in the most brutal way before replacing them with machines.**

## Chapter Two: South Asia's Unique Contradictions and Opportunities

### 2.1 Principal Contradiction: The Glow of the IT Powerhouse vs. the Dark Side of Digital Labor

India has a contradiction:

> India is renowned as an IT powerhouse (Bangalore is the "Silicon Valley of Asia"), but beneath the glow of the IT powerhouse, millions of data annotators work below minimum wage.

This contradiction can be exploited -- when international media focus on "the rise of India's AI industry," we can expose it by saying, "The foundation of India's AI industry edifice is built by workers earning 50 cents an hour."

### 2.2 Special Opportunity One: India's Democratic System and Labor Laws

Although India's democratic system has many problems, it provides:
- A legal framework for union legalization
- Constitutionally protected freedom of assembly
- An active civil society and labor rights NGOs
- An independent judiciary (public interest litigation is possible)

### 2.3 Special Opportunity Two: "Cross-Border Solidarity" of IT Professionals

India has a large number of high-paid IT professionals (programmers, data scientists). They share a natural class connection with data annotators -- although the income gap is enormous, they belong to the same industry.

Strategic objective: **Make high-paid IT professionals understand that compressing annotator costs will ultimately depress their wages too. This is an extension of the same logic.**

### 2.4 Special Opportunity Three: Global AI Safety Discussions

The world is discussing "AI safety" -- but few pay attention to the human labor conditions in AI training data.

Strategy: **Expand the "AI safety" discussion into an "AI labor ethics" discussion.** "If your AI model is trained on data annotated by exploited workers, is that AI ethical?"

## Chapter Three: Three-Phase Action Roadmap

### 3.1 Phase One: Research and Organizing (2026-2028)

**India (Main Battlefield):**

Specific tasks:
1. Conduct systematic research on India's data annotation industry -- production data, income data, working conditions data
2. Establish the "India Data Annotators Network" -- connect annotators through Telegram/WhatsApp
3. Cooperate with Indian IT unions (Trade Union Centre, IT Employees Union, etc.)
4. Launch the "Know Your Rights" campaign -- educate annotators on labor law knowledge

Key indicators:
- Establish branch organizations in 5 cities
- Cover 10,000 annotators
- Complete industry research report

**Pakistan/Bangladesh (Second Battlefield):**

Specific tasks:
1. Research the data annotation industries in Pakistan and Bangladesh
2. Establish cooperative relationships with local labor NGOs
3. Organize annotators through online platforms

### 3.2 Phase Two: Rights Struggle (2028-2032)

**Core Issue Setting:**

| Issue | Rationale | Feasibility |
|-------|-----------|-------------|
| Minimum hourly wage of $3 | Basic subsistence requirement | Medium |
| Work transparency (public pricing rules) | Prevent unilateral platform price cuts | High |
| Abolish "free training period" | Eliminate disguised unpaid labor | High |
| Data attribution rights (annotators have rights to the data they produce) | Most radical but most fundamental | Low |

**Specific Actions:**
1. Launch the "Fair Annotation Campaign" -- open letters, petitions, social media dissemination
2. Push for a "Digital Worker Classification Law" in India -- bring data annotators into formal employment relationships
3. Launch a consumer campaign against major platforms (Appen, iMerit) -- "Use ethical AI annotation services"
4. Establish "Data Annotator Cooperative" pilot -- annotators organize their own cooperatives to directly undertake annotation work

### 3.3 Phase Three: Structural Transformation (2032-2040)

**Specific Tasks:**
1. Push India to pass an "AI Workers Protection Law" (covering data annotators, content moderators, platform workers)
2. Expand the "Data Annotator Cooperative" model from India to other South Asian countries
3. Establish the "South Asia AI Labor Rights Alliance" (networking with similar organizations in Africa, Southeast Asia, and Latin America)
4. Push for inclusion of "AI training data labor conditions review" clauses in global AI ethics standards

## Chapter Four: Three Major Campaigns of South Asia

### 4.1 First Campaign: Pricing Transparency Campaign

**Goal:** Force annotation platforms to publicly disclose pricing rules, so annotators can know how each task's compensation is calculated

**Why choose this:**
- The most resonant issue (all annotators have experienced "Why is this task cheaper than that one?")
- Easiest for the public to understand ("They do the same work, why is the pay different?")
- Most likely to achieve victory (public pricing does not require platforms to spend real money, just transparency)

**Tactics:**
1. Collect pricing data from 100 annotation tasks, publish a "Pricing Black Box" report
2. Launch the social media campaign #IKnowWhatMyLaborIsWorth simultaneously
3. Submit an open letter to India's Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology
4. If platforms refuse to respond, launch a "Delayed Annotation" action

### 4.2 Second Campaign: Cooperative Alternative Campaign

**Goal:** Establish data annotator cooperatives that directly undertake annotation work, bypassing middlemen

**Model:**
```
Traditional model: Tech Giants → Outsourcing Platform → Intermediary → Team Leader → Annotator
                                                                  (Annotator gets <10%)
Cooperative model: Tech Giants → Annotator Cooperative → Annotator
                                               (Annotator gets 60-70%)
```

**Pilot Plan:**
1. Establish the first "Data Annotator Cooperative" in Bangalore
2. Cooperative membership: 50-100 annotators
3. Business sources: annotation needs of small and medium AI companies
4. Governance structure: one person, one vote; elect management
5. Profit distribution: after deducting operating costs, profits distributed to members based on labor contribution

**Key question:** How does the cooperative get its first client?
- Network through IT professional contacts
- Use public bidding platforms
- Start with low-competition small annotation tasks

### 4.3 Third Campaign: Global AI Labor Ethics Campaign

**Goal:** Incorporate "labor conditions in AI training data" into global AI ethics standards

**Why choose this:**
- The global AI governance debate is heating up (EU AI Act, UN AI governance resolutions)
- Current mainstream discussions focus on "AI alignment," "safety," "bias" -- but not on annotator labor conditions
- This is a "gap" -- precisely where to strike

**Tactics:**
1. Draft a "Global AI Labor Ethics Charter"
2. Organize data annotator endorsements in South Asia, Africa, and Southeast Asia
3. Submit to UN AI governance bodies
4. Push for inclusion of "labor conditions review" in the implementation rules of the EU AI Act

## Chapter Five: Connecting the South Asian Movement with the Global Movement

The South Asian data annotators movement has three special contributions to the global AI workers movement:

### 5.1 Technical Capacity

India has a large number of programmers and data scientists. They can provide for the global movement:
- Development of algorithm auditing tools
- Technical architecture for data trusts
- Blockchain for labor contract transparency

### 5.2 Legal Capacity

India's legal profession is vast, and its common law tradition makes its legal precedents internationally influential. Indian court rulings on "digital worker rights" can be cited in Africa and Southeast Asia.

### 5.3 Transnational Leverage

The Indian diaspora is widely distributed in the global tech industry -- Silicon Valley executives include a large number of people of Indian origin. These people belong, in class terms, to the "upper technical middle class," but retain cultural ties with India. This is a special lever -- the domestic movement in India can influence their stance within global tech companies.

## Conclusion

South Asia's AI workers are the lowest, most overlooked link in the global AI industry chain. But it is precisely this "lowest" position that gives this movement its special power:

**If the workers at the very bottom of the global AI industry chain can stand up, organize, and win rights -- this will be the most powerful blow to the entire AI capitalist system.**

"The masses of the people are the masters of history." -- South Asia's data annotators, content moderators, and digital workers are the masses of the people in the AI era. Their awakening and organization will determine the historical direction of the AI era.
