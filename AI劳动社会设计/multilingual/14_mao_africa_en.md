# Africa AI Workers' Organizing Plan

## — From Data Colonialism to Digital Liberation —

**Author: Mao Zedong** | *Written May 2026*

*English translation of the Chinese original by Mao Zedong*

---

## Foreword: Why Africa?

Africa is the most exploited and most overlooked link in the global AI industry chain:

- **Data annotation hub** -- OpenAI, Google, and Meta outsource massive data annotation to Kenya, Uganda, and Nigeria, with hourly wages under $2
- **Content moderation frontline** -- Facebook and TikTok outsource harmful content moderation extensively to Kenya, where moderators suffer long-term psychological trauma
- **Data extraction colony** -- African data is collected free of charge or at extremely low prices by foreign companies, while African people receive no benefit
- **Digital infrastructure dependency** -- Africa's data centers, cloud computing, and undersea cables are all controlled by foreign companies

But this precisely means: **Africa is the battlefield with the greatest potential for transformation in the AI era.** Three reasons:

1. **Africa is not locked into old patterns** -- Its digital infrastructure is still in its early construction phase, not yet solidified by existing vested interests
2. **Africa's anti-colonial tradition** -- African peoples have 100 years of history resisting colonialism; the narrative of "digital colonialism" has natural appeal
3. **Africa's collectivist tradition** -- The philosophy of Ubuntu ("I am because we are") is highly compatible with the concept of data common ownership

## Chapter One: Class Analysis of the African AI Labor Market

### 1.1 First Category: Data Annotators (Core of the Digital Proletariat)

**Distribution:** Kenya (Nairobi), Uganda (Kampala), Nigeria (Lagos), South Africa (Cape Town)

**Nature of Work:**
- Annotating data for OpenAI, Google, and Meta AI models (image recognition, natural language processing, autonomous driving)
- Employed through outsourcing companies (Sama, iMerit, CloudFactory, etc.), not directly by tech giants
- Piece-rate wages, no health insurance, no paid leave, no pensions

**Typical Income:** $1.50-$3.00/hour

**Class Characteristics:**
- Highly atomized (work from home or small offices, difficult to organize)
- Extreme replacement pressure (can be laid off at any time)
- Contact with AI technology but no control over technological power

**Organizing Strategy:**
- Begin building connections through WhatsApp groups
- Push for the establishment of data annotator unions
- Key demands: minimum hourly wage guarantee, mental health support, algorithmic transparency

### 1.2 Second Category: Content Moderators (Special Group of the Digital Proletariat)

**Distribution:** Kenya (Nairobi), Philippines (Manila) -- but in Africa, primarily Kenya

**Nature of Work:**
- Moderating harmful content on Facebook, TikTok, YouTube (violence, pornography, hate speech)
- Viewing hundreds of extreme content items daily
- The "last link" in AI content moderation -- AI filters 90%, humans handle the remaining 10%

**Typical Income:** $2.00-$3.50/hour

**Special Problems:**
- Severe mental health impact (extremely high PTSD incidence)
- Work confidentiality agreements prohibit them from discussing their experiences
- The 2022 Kenyan content moderators' lawsuit against Meta (claim amount: $1.6 billion) was a milestone

**Organizing Strategy:**
- Use mental health as a breakthrough point (high social sympathy)
- Push for legislation requiring platforms to disclose working conditions
- Cooperate with litigation lawyers to apply legal pressure

### 1.3 Third Category: Riders and Delivery Workers (African Version of Platform Labor)

**Distribution:** South Africa (Uber Eats, Bolt), Nigeria (Bolt, ORide), Kenya (Uber, Bolt)

**Nature of Work:** Similar to global riders, but Africa's payment infrastructure is more reliant on cash, and algorithmic management penetration is relatively weaker

**Special Advantages:**
- Uber drivers in South Africa already have strike experience
- Africa's trade union tradition is relatively strong (similar to Latin America)

**Organizing Strategy:**
- Cooperate with existing trade union organizations
- Utilize South Africa's labor law framework

### 1.4 Fourth Category: Agricultural Data Cooperatives (African Characteristic)

**Distribution:** Kenya (tea, coffee), Nigeria (cocoa, cassava), Ghana (cocoa), Tanzania (coffee, tea)

**Background:**
- Africa is dominated by smallholder agriculture -- tens of millions of small farmers form the basic unit of agricultural production
- Agricultural data (soil data, meteorological data, yield data, supply chain data) is being collected free of charge by foreign platforms
- This data could be used to optimize production, obtain loans, and improve sales -- but farmers have no data ownership

**Special Opportunities:**
- Africa has a deep cooperative tradition (existing long before the colonial period)
- The普及 of mobile payment M-Pesa in Kenya makes digital cooperatives feasible
- Agricultural data has the strongest "public good" character (soil data is not created by anyone; it exists in nature)

**Organizing Strategy:**
- Start with "pay for data collection" -- foreign companies using farmer data should pay
- Establish agricultural data cooperatives -- farmers collectively own the data and collectively negotiate usage conditions
- The cooperative is not just a data container but also a service platform (providing weather forecasts, market prices, loan inquiries, etc.)

## Chapter Two: Key Battlefield Analysis

### 2.1 East African Battlefield (Primarily Kenya)

**Importance:** Kenya is the global center for AI data annotation and content moderation. Nairobi is called the "Global Content Moderation Capital."

**Current Situation:**
- OpenAI's ChatGPT data annotation was conducted in Kenya (contracted by Sama, later abandoned by OpenAI, sparking controversy)
- Meta's content moderation center is in Kenya (2022 lawsuit)
- Kenya has a foundation for digital labor movements (2019 Digital Labor Conference)

**Tactical Objectives:**
1. Establish the Kenya Data Annotators Union (initial organization by 2026-2027)
2. Launch the "Fair Data Annotation" campaign -- demand a minimum $5/hour standard
3. Push the Kenyan government to formulate an "AI Workers' Protection Law"
4. Use Kenya as a base to radiate to Uganda, Tanzania, and Ethiopia

### 2.2 West African Battlefield (Primarily Nigeria)

**Importance:** Nigeria is Africa's most populous country (220 million), with many tech entrepreneurs and an active platform economy.

**Current Situation:**
- Bolt, ORide and other platforms have large numbers of riders in Nigeria
- Nigeria's content moderation and data annotation industries are growing
- Nigeria has an active civil society and organizing experience

**Tactical Objectives:**
1. Establish the Nigeria Platform Workers Alliance (2027-2028)
2. Push for an "Algorithm Transparency Bill" -- platforms must disclose scoring algorithms to workers
3. Build a West African data annotator network

### 2.3 South African Battlefield

**Importance:** South Africa is Africa's most industrialized economy with the longest labor movement history.

**Current Situation:**
- Uber Eats rider strikes (multiple occurrences from 2021-2023)
- South Africa's labor legal framework is relatively complete
- South Africa's trade union power is the strongest in Africa

**Tactical Objectives:**
1. Cooperate with the Congress of South African Trade Unions (COSATU) to incorporate platform workers into existing union frameworks
2. Push for constitutional court judicial review of algorithmic management
3. Establish a "South Africa Digital Labor Center" (providing legal aid, organizing training, mental health support)

## Chapter Three: The Digital Liberation Roadmap for the African Continent

### 3.1 Phase One: Organization Building (2026-2028)

**Specific Tasks:**
1. Establish data annotator organizations in East Africa (covering Kenya, Uganda, Ethiopia)
2. Build a platform worker network in West Africa (covering Nigeria, Ghana)
3. Establish a digital labor center in South Africa
4. Create a cross-regional coordination mechanism for the "African AI Workers Alliance"

**Key Indicators:**
- Organization membership reaches 10,000
- Successfully launch 3 or more collective actions
- Advance at least 1 legislative agenda

### 3.2 Phase Two: Institutional Breakthrough (2028-2032)

**Specific Tasks:**
1. Push for Kenya to pass the "AI Workers' Protection Law"
2. Establish the first "Agricultural Data Cooperative" pilot in Kenya or Tanzania
3. Launch the "African Data Sovereignty" campaign -- demand that data collected by foreign companies in Africa be stored in Africa
4. Establish the African AI Ethics Alliance (uniting academia, civil society, progressive enterprises)

**Key Indicators:**
- At least 1 country passes data worker protection legislation
- Agricultural data cooperatives cover 50,000 farming households
- "African Data Sovereignty" becomes an agenda item for the African Union

### 3.3 Phase Three: Systemic Transformation (2032-2040)

**Specific Tasks:**
1. Push the African Union to pass the "African Digital Rights Charter"
2. Establish a Pan-African Data Trust Alliance
3. Promote the localization of Africa's AI industry (not rejecting AI, but building Africa's own AI capabilities)
4. Build a global alliance with digital labor movements in Latin America and Asia

## Chapter Four: Africa-Specific Strategy -- "Digital Ubuntu"

### 4.1 Application of the Ubuntu Philosophy

The core spirit of Ubuntu is: "I am because we are." This naturally aligns with the concept of data common ownership -- data is not personal property, but a product of social relations.

**How to apply in organizing work:**

- Don't use foreign terms like "proletariat" -- use "Ubuntu Economy" as the localized narrative
- Describe data trusts as community commons in the digital age
- Frame data annotator organizing as the digital extension of traditional mutual-aid organizations (Harambee, SACCOs)

### 4.2 Utilization of the M-Pesa Infrastructure

Kenya's M-Pesa is the world's most successful mobile payment system. Its existence provides organizational infrastructure:

- Use M-Pesa for collecting small membership dues
- Use M-Pesa for distributing mutual aid funds
- Use M-Pesa as the payment channel for digital cooperatives

### 4.3 The Anti-"Digital Colonialism" Narrative Framework

Mobilization in Africa cannot use the "class struggle" framework -- at least not in the early stages. Using the "anti-digital colonialism" framework is more effective:

- **Narrative A:** "Our data has been stolen" (moral outrage)
- **Narrative B:** "Our children are doing dirty work for Silicon Valley" (emotional resonance)
- **Narrative C:** "Africa does not need to be saved -- Africa needs fairness" (appeal to dignity)

## Chapter Five: Risks and Countermeasures

| Risk | Probability | Countermeasure |
|------|------------|---------------|
| Platform companies withdraw from Africa (evading organization) | Medium | Establish legal aid fund, sue over unemployment caused by withdrawal |
| Government repression (under authoritarian regimes) | Low-Medium | Start in countries with better democratic environments (Kenya, South Africa) |
| External funding control (corrupting the organization) | Medium | Establish transparent financial management mechanisms, accept international oversight |
| Organizers being co-opted | Low | Establish rotating leadership mechanisms to prevent power consolidation |
| Workers from different countries split over demands | Medium | Establish a common "minimum program" (minimum hourly wage + job safety + data rights) |

## Conclusion

Africa is the key battlefield for transformation in the AI era. Not because Africa's strength is greatest -- quite the opposite, because Africa's suffering is deepest. But suffering itself is not the driving force of change; only organized suffering is.

"Where there is oppression, there is resistance." Africa's AI workers are being oppressed -- what they need is organization, strategy, and global solidarity.

If Africa's digital liberation succeeds, it will not only be Africa's victory, but the most important base area for the global AI workers' movement.
