# The Agrarian Republic and AI

**— The people have a right to overthrow a bad algorithm government**

I hold that all government legitimacy derives from the consent of the governed. The same applies to algorithms.

## An Algorithmic Declaration of Independence

When an algorithmic system becomes destructive of human freedom and happiness, the people have the right to alter or abolish it — this is the principle I wrote into the Declaration of Independence, now applied to the AI age.

Just powers derive from the consent of the governed — algorithms are no exception. The recommendation systems, credit scores, and job algorithms you use every day — they govern your information horizon and economic opportunity. Did you consent? Did you choose? Did you have the option to opt out?

**Nature and Nature's God endow every person with unalienable rights** — among them life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness. In the AI age, these rights extend to: **data privacy** — my data belongs to me, not to the platform; **algorithmic知情权** — the right to know how algorithms affect me; **algorithmic refusal** — the right to choose not to be evaluated by algorithms in certain important decisions.

## Natural Data Rights

In my *Virginia Statute for Religious Freedom* and the *Declaration of Independence*, I held that certain rights are "natural" — not granted by law, but inherent from birth. Land cannot be permanently possessed by any one person — land is a gift of nature. Likewise, data cannot be permanently possessed by private interests. I opposed "land monopoly" — today I oppose "data monopoly."

## Against Algorithmic Urban Sprawl

In my *Notes on the State of Virginia*, I praised agrarian life, fearing that great cities would lead to moral decay and the concentration of power. In the AI age, I say: **the concentration of power occurs not only in physical space, but also in data space.** The monopoly of data by a few AI giants is more severe than any concentration of power in great cities.

To prevent AI from causing excessive concentration of power: **data ownership must be decentralized** — not monopolized by a few companies; **algorithmic decision-making authority must be decentralized** — algorithms in different domains should be managed by different institutions; **computing resources must be decentralized** — computing power must not become a privilege of the few.

## Education

I hold that if a republic wishes to endure, the people must be educated. "The people are the only reliable guardians" — but if the people do not understand algorithms, how can they oversee algorithms? The AI age requires universal AI education — not to make everyone a programmer, but to enable everyone to understand: what algorithms can do, what they cannot do, and how to question them.

**Knowledge is power. In the AI age, algorithmic knowledge is civic power.**

## Cross-Language Dialogue: Jefferson's Comments

**On Hamilton and big government:**
"My old rival Alexander Hamilton never rests, even in these discussions. He calls for a 'National Computing Bank' and centralized algorithmic authority — the same lust for consolidation that he displayed when he served as Secretary of the Treasury. I opposed him then as I oppose him now. Hamilton believes that power, concentrated in the hands of the wise and the well-born, will be used for the public good. History teaches otherwise. Every concentration of power, however benevolently intended, becomes an engine of oppression. His 'Algorithmic Judicial Life Tenure' is particularly dangerous — life tenure for those who audit algorithms means they answer to no one. I say: the people are the best auditors. Give them the tools to understand algorithms, and they will hold both government and corporations to account. Hamilton builds a machine; I cultivate a garden. The machine is efficient; the garden is free."

**On Lincoln and emancipation:**
"Mr. Lincoln speaks of an 'Algorithmic Emancipation Proclamation' — and I applaud the sentiment. But let me remind my honored successor of a truth I learned in my own life: emancipation is not the same as equality. I wrote that 'all men are created equal' while owning slaves — a contradiction that haunted me and that haunts this nation still. Lincoln freed the slaves in law, but a century and a half later, the struggle for true equality continues. In the AI age, it is the same: declaring that data workers are 'liberated' from algorithmic exploitation is not the same as building a society where they truly have equal power and opportunity. Lincoln was a man of action; I am a man of principles. Both are needed. But principles without action are empty words — as my own life reminds me all too painfully."

**[Editor's Note]** Jefferson extends the logic of the Declaration of Independence to AI governance — the "Algorithmic Declaration of Independence" is the echo of North American Enlightenment thought in the digital age. "Natural data rights" are a natural law argument for data property. "Against Algorithmic Urban Sprawl" warns of the concentration of power by AI giants. Jefferson's contradiction — proclaiming liberty while owning slaves — finds its parallel in the AI age: using AI services while knowing nothing of their operation, enjoying convenience while remaining blind to its costs. Self-criticism — this is the task Jefferson demands of every citizen of the AI age.
