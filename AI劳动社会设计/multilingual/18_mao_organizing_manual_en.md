# Digital Workers Organizing and Mobilization Manual

## — From Atomization to Organization —

**Author: Mao Zedong** | *Written May 2026*

*English translation of the Chinese original by Mao Zedong*

---

## Foreword: Why Organize?

The fundamental problem facing digital workers is: **atomization.**

Riders run on the streets, annotators work from home, moderators remain silent under confidentiality agreements -- they do not know one another, do not know that they suffer the same hardships. Capital precisely uses this dispersal to maintain its rule.

The most important sentence in the *Selected Works of Mao Zedong* is:

> "Organize."

Just two words. But it is the starting point of all transformation.

This manual is not a theoretical article; it is a **toolbox** -- telling you how to build organizations among digital workers, from the first WhatsApp group to a nationwide union.

---

## Chapter One: Special Difficulties of Digital Worker Organizing

### 1.1 Comparison of Organizing Difficulty: Traditional Workers vs. Digital Workers

| Dimension | Traditional Factory Workers | Digital Platform Workers |
|-----------|---------------------------|------------------------|
| **Workplace** | Centralized factory | Dispersed streets/home |
| **Work hours** | Fixed shifts | Unfixed "accept orders anytime" |
| **Colleague relations** | Meet daily, have social ties | Online contact, may not even know each other |
| **Employer relationship** | Clear employment contract | "Partner," "independent contractor" |
| **Replacement pressure** | Medium (requires training) | Extremely high (can be replaced anytime) |
| **Legal protection** | Covered by labor law | Legal gray zone |

**Conclusion:** The methods used to organize traditional workers cannot be used to organize digital workers. Innovation is necessary.

### 1.2 Four Principles of Digital Organizing Work

1. **From online to offline** -- Starting with online groups is necessary, but ultimately must move offline to build genuine trust
2. **From economic to political** -- Start with the concrete (my income / my work hours), then move to the abstract (algorithms should be transparent / data should be commonly owned)
3. **From small to big** -- Start with a core group of 5 people, not a large organization of 5,000
4. **From local to global** -- Local organization is the foundation, but platforms are transnational, so organization must ultimately be transnational

## Chapter Two: Phase One -- Building Connections (Months 1-3)

### 2.1 Step One: Find the "First Batch of People"

**Do not try to organize everyone. Start with "organizers willing to organize."**

**How to find organizers:**
- Distribute flyers (with online links) at restaurants/charging stations where riders gather
- Post in data annotator forums/groups
- Spread through one or two existing contacts

**Characteristics of the first batch of organizers:**
- Dissatisfied with the industry but not despairing
- Basic communication skills
- Have time and willingness to invest
- Do not need to be heroes -- just willing to try

### 2.2 Step Two: Establish Initial Contact Network

**Tool selection:**

| Region | Preferred Tool | Backup Tool |
|--------|---------------|------------|
| Latin America | WhatsApp | Telegram, Signal |
| Africa | WhatsApp | Telegram |
| Southeast Asia | WhatsApp, Telegram | LINE (Thailand) |
| South Asia | WhatsApp, Telegram | Signal |
| Europe/North America | Signal, Telegram | WhatsApp |

**Establish multi-tier group structure:**

```
Core group (5-10 people): high-trust organizers
    │
Expanded group (50-200 people): active participants
    │
Mass group (500+ people): all workers willing to join
```

**Key principle: Do not try to build a group of 10,000 from the start.** Start with a core group of dozens, quality over quantity.

### 2.3 Step Three: First Offline Activity

**After establishing connections, arrange offline meeting opportunities as soon as possible:**

- For riders: organize a "tea break meetup" at a charging station/rest stop
- For annotators: organize a "work exchange" at a co-working space or cafe
- For content moderators: proceed cautiously (risk of confidentiality agreements), start one-on-one

**The goal of the first meeting is not to "discuss revolution" -- the goal is simply: let five to ten people sit down, get to know each other, and realize they are all troubled by the same difficulties.**

## Chapter Three: Phase Two -- Deepening Organization (Months 3-12)

### 3.1 From Groups to Cells

**A secret work method -- the Cell system:**

```
                    ┌──────┐
                    │Coord.│ (5-10 people, full/part-time organizers)
                    └──┬───┘
                       │
         ┌─────────────┼─────────────┐
         │             │             │
       ┌─┴─┐        ┌─┴─┐        ┌─┴─┐
       │Cell│........│Cell│........│Cell│  (each cell 5-10 people)
       └───┘        └───┘        └───┘
         │             │             │
       ┌─┴─┐        ┌─┴─┐        ┌─┴─┐
       │Memb│        │Memb│        │Memb│  (each cell contacts 50-100 people)
       └───┘        └───┘        └───┘
```

**Why use the cell system?**
- Security first: exposure of one cell member does not compromise the entire organization
- Management effectiveness: a person can only build deep trust relationships with a limited number (Dunbar's number: approximately 150)
- Action flexibility: cells can act independently without层层 approval

### 3.2 Preparing for Collective Action: From "Complaints" to "Demands"

**Transform scattered discontent into unified demands. Process:**

1. **Collect:** Launch a "three things you can't stand most" discussion in the group
2. **Summarize:** Organizers consolidate and identify commonalities
3. **Prioritize:** Select the "most resonant, most winnable first demand"
4. **Convert:** Turn demands into concrete, actionable requirements

**Wrong example:** "We must overthrow algorithmic exploitation!" (too abstract, no action possible)
**Correct example:** "Platforms must publicly disclose the pricing formula for each order" (concrete, actionable, verifiable)

### 3.3 First Collective Action

**Principles for the first action:**

1. **Low risk** -- Do not organize a strike for the first action. Start with an "open letter" or "petition"
2. **Quantifiable** -- Clear target ("collect 1,000 signatures within three days" is both concrete and measurable)
3. **Winnable** -- Ensure the first action can achieve a victory. Even a small win (e.g., the platform responded to the open letter)
4. **Story-worthy** -- The action must have narrative value ("Riders have stood up for the first time")

**Recommended sequence for the first action:**
1. Survey questionnaire -> 2. Internal discussion -> 3. Open letter -> 4. Social media dissemination -> 5. Request dialogue with platform management -> 6. Evaluate results

## Chapter Four: Phase Three -- Formalization (Months 12-24)

### 4.1 Choose the Form of Organization

| Form | Advantages | Disadvantages | Applicable Scenario |
|------|-----------|---------------|-------------------|
| **Informal association** | Flexible, low legal risk | Limited power, not formal enough | Early stage |
| **Registered union** | Legal recognition, bargaining rights | High registration threshold, easily controlled | Countries with good legal environments |
| **NGO/foundation** | Legal protection, easy fundraising | Easily incorporated into "depoliticized" framework | When there is financial backing |
| **Cooperative** | Economic activity participation, sustainable | Complex governance | Groups with stable income sources |
| **Digital alliance** | Cross-border ease, low cost | Weak binding force | Transnational organizing |

**There is no perfect form. Choose the one most adapted to the local legal environment.**

### 4.2 Core Elements of a Constitution

1. **Mission statement** (2-3 sentences explaining why it exists)
2. **Membership system** (who can join, rights and obligations)
3. **Decision-making mechanism** (one person one vote or representative system? What proportion needed for major issues?)
4. **Leadership elections** (term limits, rotation, recall procedures)
5. **Financial management** (transparency of dues, donations, expenditures)
6. **Discipline** (measures for violating the constitution)
7. **Procedure for amending the constitution**

### 4.3 Leadership Development

**The organization's greatest danger:** Leaders becoming detached from the masses.

**Measures to prevent bureaucratization:**
- **Term limits** -- Core leadership rotates regularly (recommended maximum 2 years)
- **Collective leadership** -- No "chairperson," establish an "executive committee"
- **Transparent decision-making** -- Major decisions require membership discussion
- **Regular self-criticism** -- Conduct "criticism and self-criticism" meetings quarterly

## Chapter Five: The Organizer's Self-Cultivation

### 5.1 Five Abilities an Organizer Must Possess

1. **The ability to listen** -- Not to educate others, but to understand what others are thinking
2. **The ability to simplify** -- Explain complex theories in one sentence
3. **The ability to lead by example** -- "Actions speak louder than words"; do it yourself first
4. **The ability to be patient** -- 90% of organizing work is trivial matters; the remaining 10% is dangerous moments
5. **Mental preparation for loss** -- Some people will quit, some will betray; efforts may not bear fruit

### 5.2 The Organizer's Eight Points of Attention

1. Speak gently, do not put on airs
2. Do not take advantage of the organization for personal gain (keep accounts open)
3. Do not engage in personality cults (power rotation)
4. Do not make decisions for the masses (guide, do not command)
5. Do not engage in secret dealings (all negotiations open and transparent)
6. Pay attention to women and minority groups (their difficulties are often deeper)
7. Do not use the organization as a stepping stone (not for personal advancement)
8. Always remember that you are one of the masses

### 5.3 Safety Precautions

**Digital security:**
- Use encrypted communication tools (Signal preferred)
- Core members use different platforms for sensitive communications
- Change passwords regularly
- Do not store membership lists in the cloud

**Personal safety:**
- Choose public spaces for offline activities
- Keep important contact information (lawyers, media, embassies)
- Understand local laws regarding unions and assembly
- Be mentally prepared for "being watched" -- but avoid excessive fear causing paralysis in the early stages

## Chapter Six: Methods of International Solidarity

### 6.1 Why International Solidarity Is Necessary

Platform capital is transnational. Grab is registered in Singapore, operates in Indonesia, and has data centers in Malaysia. If Indonesian riders go on strike, the platform can immediately dispatch orders to the Philippines.

**A single country's organization cannot resist transnational capital.** Only transnational organization can resist transnational capital.

### 6.2 Concrete Methods of International Solidarity

**Four steps from "information exchange" to "action coordination":**

1. **Information sharing (lowest form):** Organizations from different countries regularly exchange information about their own situations
2. **Mutual expressions of solidarity:** When one country's organization takes action, organizations in other countries issue statements of solidarity
3. **Coordinated action:** Within the same week, multiple countries raise similar demands to the same platform simultaneously
4. **Synchronized strike (highest form):** Strike simultaneously in multiple countries, preventing the platform from using transnational dispatch to evade the strike

### 6.3 Roadmap for Building a Global Digital Workers Network

```
Year 1: Establish country-level organizations
    ↓
Year 2: Establish regional alliances (Latin America/Africa/Southeast Asia/South Asia)
    ↓
Year 3: Establish connections between regional alliances
    ↓
Year 5: Convene the first Global Digital Workers Congress
    ↓
Year 10: Establish permanent bodies of the Global Digital Workers Alliance
```

## Conclusion

The work of organizing digital workers is perhaps one of the most difficult political tasks of the 21st century. But we have no choice.

If not organized, digital workers will forever remain "digital slaves controlled by algorithms."
If organized, they can become the "working class of the AI era," with the power to rewrite the rules.

I once said something on Jinggangshan, and today I send it to all digital worker organizers:

> "We must not only set tasks, we must also solve the problem of how to carry them out. Our task is to cross the river, but without a bridge or a boat we cannot cross. Not solving the problem of the bridge or the boat, crossing the river is an empty phrase."

This manual is that bridge, that boat. Use it to cross the river.
