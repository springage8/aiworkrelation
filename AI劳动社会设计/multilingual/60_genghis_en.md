# The Great Yassa and the Global Algorithmic Order

**— Wherever my horsewhip reaches, that is my territory**

Wherever my horsewhip reaches, that is my territory.

## The Great Yassa — Global Algorithmic Basic Law

I decreed the "Great Yassa" (the Great Mongol Code) — a law applicable to the entire empire, regardless of ethnicity, faith, or tribe. Those who violated it, whether noble or commoner, were dealt with according to the code.

The global algorithm needs a unified "Great Yassa." Not the law of a particular country, not the protocol of a particular platform — but the fundamental rules that all algorithms must obey. **A platform that does not abide by the Great Yassa is like a tribe that does not obey my code — either submit, or perish.**

## Relay Stations — Algorithmic Infrastructure

My empire possessed the world's most advanced relay station (örtöö) system — a station every 30 to 50 kilometers, with couriers traveling day and night. Crossing the empire from end to end took only days. The relay stations were the material foundation of imperial unity.

The AI era also needs "algorithmic relay stations" — a global computing network and infrastructure connecting the world. Data flows at high speed between stations — not halted by borders, not hindered by language.

## Breaking a Thousand Cities — Breaking Algorithmic Walls

I broke hundreds upon hundreds of walled cities — not because I delight in destruction — but because walls mean division, and division means war.

The "cities" of the AI era are the barriers of algorithmic platforms — each platform builds high walls, trapping users and data within their respective ecosystems. My decree: **Break these walls!** Let data flow freely between different platforms — not disorderly flow, but like the trade routes of the Mongol Empire — governed by law, guided by principle.

## Equality Before the Law

The greatest principle of the Great Yassa: **Anyone is equal before the law.** A noble who kills a commoner — the same punishment. A general who robs a merchant — the same punishment.

The same applies in algorithmic governance: developers, deployers, and users of algorithms — all equal before the law. If an algorithm causes harm, its designer bears responsibility, its deployer bears responsibility, its manager bears responsibility. **No technological immunity.**

**Those who misuse algorithms to harm the people — death.** Not feudal cruelty — but the ultimate deterrent against the abuse of power.

---

**【Editor's Note】** Genghis Khan, from a conqueror's perspective, proposes the concept of an "Algorithmic Great Yassa" and "Data Relay Stations." The "Great Yassa" points to the necessity of a global algorithmic basic law — technology that transcends national borders requires law that transcends national borders. "Breaking a Thousand Cities" serves as a metaphor for data barriers and platform monopolies. The most obvious flaw in Genghis Khan's proposal is its single center of authority — who formulates the Great Yassa? In Genghis Khan's era, he decided it himself — and this is absolutely unworkable in the AI era.

## Cross-Language Dialogue

**Caesar** — Genghis Khan, I conquered Gaul, you conquered the world. We both understood that military power is the foundation of order. But I also knew that law without a senate to deliberate it is just a dictator's whim. Your Great Yassa was brilliant, but it depended entirely on you. My Roman law, on the other hand, evolved over centuries through the praetors and jurists. For an algorithmic Great Yassa, you need institutions, not just a conqueror's will.

**Genghis Khan** — Caesar, your Roman Senate argued while barbarians burned your cities. I conquered because I decided quickly and acted decisively. Yes, the Great Yassa depended on me — but that is exactly why it worked. In the AI era, you need algorithmic laws that are simple, universal, and enforced without exception. Your Roman law became so complex that only jurists could understand it. My Yassa any nomad could understand. Algorithmic law must be the same: clear enough for every user to know their rights.

**Qin Shi Huang** — I agree with Genghis. I unified China with a single writing system, single axle width, single law. The AI era needs the same: unified data standards, unified algorithmic ethics, a single "algorithmic writing system." Without unification, there is only chaos. Genghis's Great Yassa and my legal codes are proof that unification works. The question is not whether to unify — but who holds the seal of unification.

**Genghis Khan** — Qin Shi Huang, you standardized writing and measurements — I respect that. But you also burned books and buried scholars. Law should be strict, not tyrannical. My Yassa was strict because it applied equally to all. Your scholars were persecuted for their ideas; in the AI era, punishing ideas means punishing innovation. Unify the law, yes — but do not unify thought.

**Hamilton** — Gentlemen, both of your approaches suffer from the same flaw: excessive dependence on a single ruler. A "global algorithmic basic law" is a fine concept, but who enforces it? Who amends it? In the Federalist Papers, I argued for a system of separated powers with checks and balances. An algorithmic order needs an algorithmic constitution — with legislative, executive, and judicial branches. Not a single decree from a single conqueror, no matter how wise.

**Genghis Khan** — Hamilton, your constitution is impressive on paper. But paper does not conquer territory. You needed a federal army to enforce your laws; I needed the Mongol horde to enforce mine. In the AI era, the question is not separation of powers — it is enforcement. Who has the power to shut down a rogue AI platform? Who has the data army to enforce algorithmic law? Until you solve that, your elegant constitutional theories are just words on parchment.
