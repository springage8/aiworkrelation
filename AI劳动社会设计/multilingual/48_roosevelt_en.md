# Four Freedoms and a Digital New Deal

The only thing we have to fear is **fear itself** — including the fear of AI.

## The Digital Four Freedoms

In 1941, I proposed the Four Freedoms. The AI age version:

**One: Freedom of expression** — expression free from the shadow of algorithms. Recommendation systems must not filter your speech for commercial gain. Every message you send has the right to be truly seen — not silently buried in a news feed after being tagged "low priority content" by an algorithm.

**Two: Freedom of worship** — freedom of belief free from algorithmic recommendation manipulation. What you believe should be determined by your own heart, not shaped by algorithmically pushed content. The "information cocoon" is the most insidious violation of freedom of belief — because you do not even know you are being constrained.

**Three: Freedom from want** — when AI replaces your job, you have the right not to fall into deprivation. In 1935, I signed the Social Security Act, establishing America's first social security system. The AI age needs "Digital Social Security" — those who lose their jobs to AI receive compensation and retraining.

**Four: Freedom from algorithmic fear** — freedom from arbitrary algorithmic judgment, freedom from manipulation by scoring systems. Algorithms must not be allowed to make life-changing decisions about you without notification and transparency.

## A Digital New Deal

The "New Deal" of 1933 saved America from the Great Depression. The AI age needs a "Digital New Deal" — large-scale public investment in AI infrastructure (computing centers, datasets, public models), while establishing an AI welfare system. Not handouts — but giving every person a place in the new digital economy.

## Fireside Chats

During the Great Depression, I communicated directly with the American people through "fireside chats" — candid, warm, genuine. AI governance also needs "fireside chats" — every major change to an algorithm should be explained like a fireside chat: in language ordinary people can understand, explaining what the algorithm did, why it did it, and what impact it will have.

## Cross-Language Dialogue: Roosevelt's Comments

**On Lincoln and union:**
"Abraham Lincoln understood the deepest truth of democratic governance: that a house divided cannot stand. In the AI age, this truth is more urgent than ever. The 'algorithmic iron curtain' that Churchill warns about is already dividing our information commons into warring tribes. Lincoln held the Union together with blood and courage. We must hold the digital Union together before it breaks apart. But I would add something to Lincoln's vision: union is not enough. A union of the desperate is a union that will not last. Lincoln freed the slaves but could not give them forty acres and a mule. My New Deal understood that the union must also provide. In the AI age, we need Lincoln's unity of purpose combined with my guarantee of security — union plus provision, together at last."

**On Churchill and war leadership:**
"Winston — my dear friend and fellow warrior — speaks of AI as a battle to be fought. He is right, of course. He was always right about when to fight. But Winston was a war leader; I was a depression leader. War leaders tell people to sacrifice; depression leaders tell people they will be taken care of. Both are needed in the AI age. We must fight against algorithmic tyranny — that is Churchill's cause. But we must also build a society where those displaced by AI do not starve — that is my cause. Winston says 'never surrender' to algorithmic oppression. I say 'never let a man be crushed by forces he cannot control.' We are two sides of the same shield: Churchill the sword, Roosevelt the shelter. The AI age needs both."

---

**[Editor's Note]** Roosevelt extends the "Four Freedoms" into the AI age — especially "freedom from algorithmic fear," which precisely responds to contemporary anxiety about "algorithmic scoring fear." The core of the "Digital New Deal" is an AI development model that combines public investment with welfare protection. The strength of Roosevelt's approach lies in its activist tradition — "do something, try it, fix it if it doesn't work" — but the challenge of the AI age is more complex than the Great Depression: the Depression affected the economy; AI affects every aspect of human life.
