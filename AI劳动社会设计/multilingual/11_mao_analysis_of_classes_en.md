## I. Core Thesis

> **"Who are our enemies? Who are our friends? This is a question of the first importance for the revolution."**
> — Mao Zedong, *Analysis of the Classes in Chinese Society* (1925)

The transformative forces of the AI era have already taken shape, but what is lacking is a clear **class analysis framework**. Without this, all institutional design is shooting in the dark.

Existing documents 07 and 09 design a complete set of institutions. But they never answer: **Who drives this change? Who opposes it? Who can be won over?**

This document fills that gap.

## III. ENEMIES: Forces Resisting Change

### Class 1: AI Monopoly Capital

**Includes:** Owners, shareholders, and core executives of large AI platforms (Sam Altman of OpenAI, Pichai of Google, Zuckerberg of Meta, Zhang Yiming of ByteDance, and their venture capital backers)

**Economic position:** Controls the three most essential means of production in the AI era—**data** (user behavior data pools), **computing power** (GPU clusters and data centers), and **algorithms** (foundation models and recommendation systems)

**Global annual revenue (top 5 AI platforms):** Over $1.5 trillion—equivalent to the GDP of a medium-sized developed country.

**Class characteristics:**
- Monopoly degree higher than industrial-era trusts—network effects + data flywheels + ecosystem lock-in create "winner-takes-all"
- Controls distribution—decides who is seen (recommendation algorithms), who is trusted (credit scores), who is hired (AI interviews)
- Controls infrastructure—from chips (NVIDIA) to cloud (AWS/Azure/GCP) to models (GPT/Claude/Gemini)

**Political attitude:** Actively opposes data common ownership, algorithm transparency, and AI redistribution. Uses lobbying, academic funding, and media control to shape the narrative that "AI risk requires corporate self-regulation."

**Strategy:**
- They are the principal aspect of the contradiction, but the current balance of forces is overwhelmingly in their favor
- Direct confrontation will fail—exploit their internal contradictions (market competition, technical路线 disagreements)
- As Lenin noted, "there are contradictions within every imperialist camp"—NVIDIA and its customers (OpenAI, Google) have conflicting interests

---

### Class 2: Military-AI Complex

**Includes:** Military AI contractors (Palantir, Anduril, Raytheon, Lockheed Martin), military AI R&D institutions, research systems dependent on military budgets

**Economic position:** Global military AI market projected at $300 billion by 2030. Profit margins (30-50%) far exceed civilian AI.

**Class characteristics:**
- Business model depends on conflict and war—peace is against their interests
- Deep integration with government—the "revolving door" (former Defense Secretary → AI company CEO → back to government setting AI policy)
- Minimal ethical constraints—military AI has no problem of "user consent"

**Strategy:**
- Morally isolate them (but don't expect them to change position)
- Expose their methods of manipulating policy and public opinion
- The decisive battle: Can we stop the full deployment of **Lethal Autonomous Weapons Systems (LAWS)**?

## IV. THE MIDDLE: Forces That Can Be Won Over

### Class 3: The Tech Middle Class — *the critical variable*

This is the most complex and most important group. It needs subdivision:

#### 3.1 Upper Tech Elite (algorithm engineers, AI researchers, product managers)

**Economic position:**
- Annual income: Silicon Valley $300K-$800K; global South $50K-$150K
- Possesses human capital in the Marxian sense—market scarcity gives them bargaining power
- But **does not own the means of production**—code belongs to the company, models belong to the company, data belongs to the company

**Political attitude:** Vacillating middle
- Algorithm engineers worship "technological neutrality," uninterested in politics
- But increasingly divided on "who to work for"—more AI researchers refuse to work for military contractors
- If mass layoffs hit (AI replacing AI programmers), attitudes will sharply "left-turn"

**Strategy:**
- Educate them: "technology is not neutral" (Foucault's knowledge-power analysis)
- Awaken their professional ethics: "every line of code you write has social consequences"
- Exploit their contradiction with capital—when capital decides to replace *them* with AI, they awaken

#### 3.2 Lower Tech Workers (IT support, data analysts, junior developers)

**Economic position:**
- Annual income: developed world $50K-$150K; global South $20K-$50K
- Very high AI risk to job stability—大量 repetitive coding and data processing will be automated
- Lacks the irreplaceability of the tech elite

**Political attitude:** Potentially radical—they are the most likely within the tech middle class to be replaced by AI, and the most receptive to change

**Strategy:** Key recruitment target. They are the bridge between the tech middle class and the digital proletariat.

### Class 4: Traditional Capital

**Includes:** Non-AI traditional capitalists—manufacturing, retail, real estate, traditional finance

**Economic position:** Being eroded and squeezed by AI capital. Retail destroyed by Amazon, finance impacted by algorithmic trading, manufacturing hit by AI automation.

**Political attitude:** Complex and contradictory
- They have **competitive contradictions** with AI monopoly capital—exploitable
- But they are still in an **exploitative relationship** with labor—not trustworthy
- On specific issues they may be "temporary fellow travelers"—e.g., supporting data privacy (because *their* data is also collected by AI platforms)

**Strategy:**
- Use their contradictions with AI capital where it doesn't harm labor's fundamental interests
- But never treat them as reliable allies—when their own exploitative interests are threatened, they will immediately betray

## V. FRIENDS: The Basic Forces of Change

### Class 5: The Digital Proletariat — *the main revolutionary force*

#### 5.1 Gig Workers (riders, ride-hailing drivers, delivery workers)

**Global scale:** ~500-800 million people (including part-time), fastest-growing labor group

**Economic position:**
- No formal employment relationship—legally "independent contractors," actually fully controlled by algorithms
- No labor protections—no healthcare, pension, paid leave
- Income often below minimum wage (after platform commission)
- No bargaining power—one point lower on rating, half the orders

**Class characteristics:**
- Most directly and significantly exploited by algorithms—every delivery route, every minute of waiting time is optimized to the extreme
- Atomized—workers have no connection with each other, competing against each other
- But beginning to organize—Chinese food delivery rider strikes, Uber driver unions in the US, Deliveroo class actions in the UK

**Political attitude:** Extremely dissatisfied with status quo, but need political direction

**Strategy:**
- This is the most revolutionary force—they are the most exploited, with nothing to lose
- Help them organize—break through the platform's atomization strategy
- Help them move from "interest struggle" (higher pay) to "system struggle" (algorithm fairness, data rights)
- Typical entry point: **"algorithm strikes"**—collective offline, collective bad ratings, collective data poisoning

#### 5.2 Digital Manual Laborers (data annotators, content moderators, AI trainers)

**Global scale:** ~50-100 million people

**Economic position:**
- Bottom of the global division of labor—annotators in Kenya at $2/hour, Philippines at $1.50/hour
- Severe psychological trauma—content moderators continuously view violence, child abuse, terrorist content; PTSD rates extremely high
- Completely invisible—users don't know "what trains ChatGPT isn't algorithms, it's cheap labor in Kenya"

**Strategy:**
- Long-term goal: eliminate "digital sweatshops"
- Medium-term: transparency—demand that AI companies disclose annotator labor conditions and compensation
- Short-term: their contradiction with AI monopoly capital is irreconcilable—the problem is they have no channel to be heard

### Class 6: The Marginalized and Replaced

**Includes:** Workers replaced by AI—translators, customer service, junior accountants, junior lawyers, illustrators...

**Scale and trend:**
- 2023-2030: 300-500 million jobs globally projected to be directly replaced by AI (Goldman Sachs, McKinsey, multiple sources)
- And the replacement speed far exceeds previous industrial revolutions

**Political attitude:** Angry but directionless—easily attracted to populism and xenophobia

**Strategy:**
- This is the most critical and most dangerous group
- If their anger is not channeled in the right direction (against AI monopoly capital), it will be channeled in the wrong direction (anti-immigrant, anti-globalization, racist)
- The correct direction: **"AI didn't take your job—capital used AI to take your job"**

## VII. Strategic Application

### 7.1 Who is the main enemy?

**Main enemy at current stage = AI Monopoly Capital**

Not "all capitalists," not "AI technology itself." Making this clear is crucial. If the target is set too broadly ("eliminate capitalism"), it has no feasibility given the current balance of forces. If set too narrowly ("make AI fairer"), it will be absorbed as reformism.

The correct medium-term objective: **data common ownership**—the limit of what capitalism can accept (analogous to public libraries, public education), and the starting point for deeper change.

### 7.2 Who are the main allies?

**The digital proletariat (gig workers + digital manual laborers) is the basic force.**
**The lower layer of the tech middle class is the main ally to be won.**

### 7.3 Whose contradictions can be exploited?

- **AI Monopoly Capital vs Traditional Capital**—common interest on "data monopoly" issues
- **Within AI Monopoly Capital**—large platforms vs small companies, model companies vs computing providers (NVIDIA)
- **AI Monopoly Capital vs National Sovereignty**—especially in Latin America, Europe—states have motivation to resist US AI hegemony

### 7.4 Current action guidelines

```
Strategic direction: Data commonization (not eliminating capital, but drawing boundaries)
Main contradiction: AI monopoly capital vs all of society
Main force: Digital proletariat + lower tech middle class
United front: Anti-monopoly forces in traditional capital + sovereign states + Global South
Forms of struggle: Legislative (algorithm transparency laws)
                  + Economic (data dividends)
                  + Organizational (digital unions)
```
