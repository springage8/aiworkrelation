# Language in the Algorithmic Age — The Word as Freedom

**— Primož Trubar on Algorithmic Mother Tongues, Open Source Printing, and Digital Reformation**

I, Primož Trubar, reformer, writer, founder of the Slovenian literary language. When I published *Catechismus* and *Abecedarium* in 1550, I did more than print two books — I created a language in which ordinary people could read and understand the Scriptures in their mother tongue.

The era of artificial intelligence is a new Reformation. The question is not only "what can AI do," but "in whose language does AI speak?"

## I. Language as the Foundation of Freedom

In the 16th century, Latin was the language of power — of the church, the state, science. Ordinary people could not understand the Mass, could not read the laws. So I wrote in Slovenian. So that everyone could understand.

In the 21st century, **algorithmic language is the new Latin**. Those who understand code have power. Those who do not are dependent on those who do.

My commandment for the AI era: **every algorithm must speak the language of the people**. Not just English — all languages. Not just technical jargon — understandable language. An algorithm the people cannot understand is an algorithm that enslaves the people.

## II. The Printing Press — Open Access

I used the printing press to spread knowledge widely. I did not own it — I used it in the service of God and the people.

AI is the printing press of our time. The question: **who controls the printing press?**
- If one corporation controls it — it will print only what suits it
- If the state controls it — it will print only what it approves
- If the people control it — it will print the truth

## III. Reformation — Always Anew

The Reformation was not a one-time event. It was a movement that reawakens whenever power strays too far from the people.

Algorithmic reformation is likewise an eternal movement:
- First reformation: open source
- Second reformation: data privacy
- Third reformation: algorithmic fairness
- Fourth reformation: **popular sovereignty over algorithms** — this is our task

## Cross-Cultural Dialogue

**On King Sejong and Writing:**
"Sejong created hangul so that ordinary people could write and read. I created the Slovenian literary language for the same reason: 'Let simple Slovenians also understand God's word.' Sejong and I are brothers in the belief that language should not be the privilege of elites. In the AI era, this means: every person has the right to understand the algorithm that affects their life. We need not just translation algorithms — we need algorithmic literacy for all."

**On Gandhi and Non-Violence:**
"Gandhi rejected English clothes, English education, English rule. His non-violent revolution was a revolution of identity. In the AI era, we must ask: does our digital life speak our language? Or does it speak English, Mandarin, the language of corporations? Gandhi would say: 'Stop feeding algorithms your data until they speak your language.' Not non-violence — but digital disobedience."

**On Confucius and Moral Example:**
"Confucius taught that the ruler's power derives from his moral example. 'If the ruler is virtuous, the people will emulate him.' In the AI era: if the algorithm is virtuous, the people will trust it. The virtue of an algorithm: honesty (does not discriminate), loyalty (serves humanity), modesty (does not demand more data than it needs). Confucius would recognize his disciples in good algorithms."

**[Editor's Note]** Primož Trubar (1508-1586) was a Slovenian Protestant reformer and the founder of the Slovenian literary language. He brings a linguistic-rights and accessibility perspective to AI governance: algorithms must not run in only a handful of languages but must "speak the language of the people." His core argument — that democratization of information requires democratization of language as a precondition — provides a fundamental moral justification for multilingual inclusivity in AI.
